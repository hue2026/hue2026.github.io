by the end of today, you'll be able to answer that question three
different ways."

"Today we master Lesson 4: solving systems of equations using GRAPHING,
SUBSTITUTION, and ELIMINATION — and then applying all three to real-world
problems that show up on the Regents and in real life. Two equations, two
unknowns, one solution. Let's get into it."

"SECOND — about 18 minutes on the GRAPHING method. We plot both lines,

💡 [Deliver this slowly — it's the big idea of the whole unit]:
"Here is the one sentence to carry through the entire hour. A single
linear equation, like y = 2x + 1, has INFINITELY many solutions — every
point on its line works. But the moment you demand that TWO equations be
true at the SAME TIME, you usually pin it down to exactly ONE point. The
solution to a system isn't a line — it's the single place where two lines
agree. Everything today is just three different ways to hunt down that
one agreement."

SLIDE 3 — DO NOW & KEY VOCABULARY 🔑
[Duration: ~6 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Put the Do Now on screen. Give genuine silent work time. Then build the
vocabulary, pointing to each card.]

"Do Now — in your notebook, no calculator. Is the point (2, 3) a solution
to BOTH of these equations? Test it and show your work:
        y = x + 1     AND     y = 2x − 1"

[Give 90 seconds. Circulate. Watch for students testing only one
equation — gently nudge: 'You proved it works for one… does it work for
the OTHER?']

✋ "Who'll walk us through it? Plug x = 2, y = 3 into the first one."
   [Expected: 3 = 2 + 1 ✓. Then the second: 3 = 2(2) − 1 = 3 ✓.]

"Beautiful. It satisfies BOTH equations, so (2, 3) is THE solution to
this system. And notice what you just did instinctively — you checked
both. That habit alone will save you points on the Regents."

💡 [The geometric meaning — this is the conceptual heart of the Do Now]:
"Here's what's secretly happening. An equation like y = x + 1 is a whole
LINE — a road made of infinitely many points. The point (2, 3) being a
solution to BOTH equations means it sits on BOTH roads at once. There's
only one place two straight roads can cross. So the solution to a system
is literally the INTERSECTION POINT of the two lines. Algebra and
geometry are telling you the same story in two languages."

"Now let's own four words. Say each one after me."

"WORD 1: SYSTEM OF EQUATIONS
Two or more equations sharing the SAME variables. The key phrase is 'same
variables' — the x in one equation means the exact same x in the other.
That's what links them into a system instead of two unrelated problems."

"WORD 2: SOLUTION OF A SYSTEM
The ordered pair (x, y) that makes EVERY equation true at the same time.
Not 'mostly true.' Not 'true for one.' True for ALL of them,
simultaneously. Geometrically — the intersection point."

"WORD 3: CONSISTENT
A system that HAS at least one solution. The lines either cross once, or
they're secretly the same line. Consistent means 'there's an answer to
find.'"

"WORD 4: INCONSISTENT
A system with NO solution. The lines are parallel — same steepness,
different heights — so they never, ever meet. Inconsistent means 'no
agreement is possible.'"

✋ [Fast check — point to random students]:
   "What's the key phrase in 'system of equations'?" (same variables)
   "Where do you find the solution on a graph?" (the intersection)
   "Consistent or inconsistent — lines that never meet?" (inconsistent)

SLIDE 4 — WHAT IS A SYSTEM? THE INTERSECTION 📈
[Duration: ~5 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the diagram: two lines crossing at (3, 4) — one solid, one
dashed. Point to the intersection circle.]

"Look at this graph. We have two lines: y = x + 1, solid, and y = −x + 7,
dashed. They cross at one point — (3, 4)."

✋ "Here's my question: what makes the point (3, 4) so special? Why can't
   I just grab any other point on the solid line and call it the solution
   to the system? Talk to a neighbor for 20 seconds."

[Reveal:] "Because every other point on the solid line satisfies only the
FIRST equation. The point (3, 4) is the ONLY point that lives on BOTH
lines — it's the only place the two rules agree."

💡 [DEEP CONCEPT — the metaphor that makes it click]:
"Think of a system as a competition between two RULES. Each equation is a
constraint — a demand. Equation one says 'you must be on my line.'
Equation two says 'no, you must be on MY line.' The solution is the one
point obedient enough to satisfy both demands at once. It's like two GPS
routes from two different friends meeting at the same street corner —
that corner is the answer. Write this down: SOLUTION = INTERSECTION
POINT."

⚠️ [REGENTS TRAP — say it with weight]:
"Here is a classic Regents trap. A solution must satisfy BOTH equations.
Students plug their answer into one equation, see it works, and stop. Do
NOT stop. Always substitute your (x, y) back into BOTH original equations.
If it only works for one, it is not the solution to the system."

SLIDE 6 — METHOD 1: THE GRAPHING METHOD 📊
[Duration: ~7 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the example system and the four-step box. Then work the graph on
the diagram, step by step.]

"Method one — graphing. We literally draw both lines and find where they
cross. Our example:
        y = 2x − 1
        y = −x + 5"

✋ "Before we plot anything — one line has slope 2 starting at (0, −1),
   the other has slope −1 starting at (0, 5). One climbs, one falls. Can
   you guess roughly WHERE they'll meet, just from those numbers? 15
   seconds."
   [Don't confirm yet — let them commit to a guess. Curiosity primes
   memory.]

"Here's the four-step recipe — write it down:
  Step 1 — Rewrite both equations in y = mx + b form.
  Step 2 — Plot the y-intercept (0, b) for each line.
  Step 3 — Use the slope as rise/run to plot more points.
  Step 4 — Find the intersection — that's your solution!"

[Now build the graph on screen.]

"Line one: y = 2x − 1. Start at (0, −1). Slope 2 means up 2, right 1.
Line two: y = −x + 5. Start at (0, 5). Slope −1 means down 1, right 1.
Trace them out… and they cross at (2, 3)."

"Let's verify like professionals — plug into BOTH originals:
  y = 2x − 1 →  3 = 2(2) − 1 = 3 ✓
  y = −x + 5 →  3 = −(2) + 5 = 3 ✓
Confirmed. The solution is (2, 3)."

💡 [The honest limitation of graphing]:
"Now, graphing is wonderful for SEEING the answer — it builds your
intuition. But it has a real weakness. What if the lines crossed at
(2.375, 1.8)? You could never read that precisely off hand-drawn graph
paper. So here's the rule: graphing is your tool for VISUAL insight and
clean integer answers. When you need EXACT values, especially fractions,
you switch to algebra. That's exactly why substitution and elimination
exist — and we'll get there."

⚠️ [REGENTS NOTE]:
"On the Regents, if a graphing problem gives a calculator, Desmos-style
plotting is fast and exact. But if it's a non-calculator section with
ugly numbers, don't fight the graph — reach for algebra."

SLIDE 7 — THE THREE PERSONALITIES OF SYSTEMS 🎭
[Duration: ~6 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the three-panel diagram: intersecting, parallel, and same-line.
Give each its own beat.]

"Not every system has one neat answer. Systems come in three
'personalities,' and you can predict which one you've got just by
comparing slopes and y-intercepts — without solving anything."

"PERSONALITY 1 — CONSISTENT & INDEPENDENT (one solution):
The lines have DIFFERENT slopes, so they cross at exactly one point. One
solution. Example: y = x + 2 and y = 2x + 1 meet at (1, 3). This is the
'normal' case — most systems you'll see."

"PERSONALITY 2 — INCONSISTENT (no solution):
SAME slope, DIFFERENT y-intercept. The lines are parallel — equally
steep, but at different heights — so they run forever and never touch.
Zero solutions. Example: y = x + 2 and y = x + 3. Same slope of 1, but
one's higher. They'll never meet."

"PERSONALITY 3 — CONSISTENT & DEPENDENT (infinite solutions):
SAME slope, SAME y-intercept. They're secretly the SAME line, just dressed
up differently. Every point on the line works — infinitely many
solutions. Example: y = x + 2 and 2y = 2x + 4 are the identical line."

"So here's your QUICK CLASSIFIER — memorize this:
  • Different slopes  →  1 solution (they intersect)
  • Same slope, different y-int  →  0 solutions (parallel)
  • Same slope, same y-int  →  infinite solutions (same line)"

✋ "Real-world test. I'll describe a scenario; you call out the type. Two
   phone plans that ALWAYS cost the same amount, no matter how many
   minutes you use." [Same line → infinite/dependent.]
   "Now: two plans that can NEVER cost the same, no matter what."
   [Parallel → inconsistent, no solution.]

⚠️ [REGENTS TRAP — this one is subtle]:
"Careful with the word 'inconsistent.' It does NOT mean one of the
equations is wrong. Each equation is a perfectly valid line! It's the
SYSTEM that has no solution, because the two lines never share a point.
The Regents loves testing whether you understand that distinction."
