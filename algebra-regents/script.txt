[Duration: ~3 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[As students settle in, display the title slide: "Lesson 3 · Graphing
Linear Functions." Begin with energy and a sense of intrigue. Stand
near the screen.]

"Good morning / afternoon, everyone! Let me start with a question that
sounds simple but is secretly powerful. Ready? Here it is: if I told you
ONE number for where a line starts, and ONE number for how it moves —
just two numbers — could you draw the entire line, stretching forever in
both directions? Every point on it, perfectly?"

[Pause. Let students react — some will say no, some will hesitate.]

"The answer is yes. Two numbers. That's it. By the end of today you will
look at an equation like y = 2x + 1 and SEE the line in your head before
your pencil ever touches paper. That's a kind of superpower, and it shows
up on the Regents exam more than almost any other skill."

"Today we're studying the single most important shape in all of algebra:
the straight line. We'll decode what makes a function 'linear,' we'll
master slope and y-intercept, we'll graph any line using y = mx + b, and
we'll use a real NYC taxi ride to prove this isn't abstract — it's the
math behind the price of everything you buy by the unit. Let's get into
it."

💡 [DEEP CONTEXT — deliver this slowly and deliberately]:
"Here's the big picture. A linear function is the math of constant change.
Not change that speeds up, not change that slows down — change that
happens at exactly the same rate, every single step. That's rarer and
more beautiful than it sounds. When your phone plan charges the same per
gigabyte, when a candle burns the same per hour, when a taxi charges the
same per mile — that steady, predictable rate is what draws a straight
line. So when you learn to read a line, you're really learning to read
any steady relationship in the world."

SLIDE 3 — DO NOW & KEY VOCABULARY 🔑
[Duration: ~6 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the Do Now problem and the vocabulary cards side by side. Give
students real silent work time on the Do Now FIRST, then debrief.]

"Do Now — in your notebook, solve this for y:  2x + 3y = 6. One rule:
you must get y all alone on one side. Take 90 seconds. Go."

[Give a genuine 90 seconds. Circulate. Look for who subtracts first and
who tries to divide first.]

✋ [Debrief by building it live on the board, narrating every move:]
"Let's walk it. We start with 2x + 3y = 6. Step one — subtract 2x from
both sides to get the y-term alone: 3y = -2x + 6. Step two — divide
EVERY term by 3: y = (-2/3)x + 2. Done. Notice I divided all three
pieces by 3, not just one. That's the most common place people slip."

💡 "Look at what just happened — that's not just 'solving.' We
transformed the equation into a special outfit called slope-intercept
form. The same line, the same truth, but now dressed so we can read its
two secret numbers instantly. Hold that — we'll name those numbers in
ten seconds."

[Now point to the three vocabulary cards. Have students say each term
aloud after you.]

"Three words to own before we go further. Say each one after me."

"WORD 1: LINEAR FUNCTION 📈
A relationship where the rate of change is CONSTANT — the same every
step. Its graph is always a perfectly straight line, never a curve.
Constant rate is the whole secret. That's the test for 'linear.'"

"WORD 2: SLOPE (m) ⛰️
How steep the line is, calculated as RISE over RUN — the change in y
divided by the change in x. Slope is the line's rate of change. It is
the single most important number in this entire unit."

"WORD 3: Y-INTERCEPT (b) 🎯
Where the line crosses the y-axis. It's the starting value — the value
of y when x = 0. Think of it as the line's home base, where everything
begins before any movement happens."

[Point to the formula box.]
"And here's the equation that ties them together: y = mx + b. The m is
the slope. The b is the y-intercept. That's the whole code."

✋ [Quick check — fire these fast at random students:]
"In our Do Now answer, y = (-2/3)x + 2 — what's the slope? What's the
y-intercept? Where on the graph would you put your pencil FIRST?"
   [Expected: slope = -2/3, y-intercept = 2, start at the point (0, 2).
   Affirm warmly, correct gently.]

SLIDE 4 — WHAT IS SLOPE? RISE OVER RUN 📐
[Duration: ~6 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the graph showing two points with the rise and run marked.
Point physically to the rise, then the run, as you speak.]

✋ [Open with the Socratic prompt on the slide:]
"Look at this line. Imagine you're walking along it from LEFT to RIGHT.
Are you walking uphill or downhill? And how steep is it compared to a
staircase in your home? Tell me."
   [Take a few answers. Affirm "uphill" — this line rises.]

"Slope answers a precise question: for every 1 unit you move RIGHT along
the x-axis, how many units do you move UP or DOWN? On this graph, we rise
60 and run 90, so the slope is 60 over 90, which simplifies to 2/3."

[Point to the formula box: m = rise/run = (y₂ - y₁)/(x₂ - x₁).]

💡 [DEEP CONCEPT — deliver deliberately]:
"Here's why slope deserves to be called the 'personality' of a line.
Slope alone tells you everything about how a line behaves — its
direction, its steepness, whether it's climbing gently or shooting up
fast. Two lines can start at totally different places, but if they have
the same slope, they move identically forever. Slope is the line's
character. The y-intercept is just where it happens to start."

[Point to the Memory Trick card.]
"Memory trick to lock in forever: RISE is vertical — up and down. RUN is
horizontal — left and right. And rise comes BEFORE run, just like y
comes before x in the coordinate pair. Rise over run. Say it three times
in your head."

✋ "On paper, find the slope between the points (1, 2) and (4, 8). Thirty
   seconds."
   [Reveal: rise = 8 - 2 = 6, run = 4 - 1 = 3, slope = 6/3 = 2. Affirm
   the SUBTRACTION step — that's where errors hide.]

SLIDE 5 — THE FOUR SLOPE PERSONALITIES ⛰️📉➖🧱
[Duration: ~6 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the four-quadrant slope diagram: positive, negative, zero,
undefined. Give each its own dramatic beat and a hand gesture.]

"A line can have exactly four kinds of slope — four personalities. Let's
meet them, and I want you to make the shape with your arm as we go."

"PERSONALITY 1 — POSITIVE (m > 0): RISING left to right. Tilt your arm
uphill. Real life: a price increasing, a temperature climbing."

"PERSONALITY 2 — NEGATIVE (m < 0): FALLING left to right. Tilt your arm
downhill. Real life: a cost decreasing, time running out, a candle
burning down."

"PERSONALITY 3 — ZERO (m = 0): FLAT. Arm straight out, horizontal. No
change at all — a constant value. The line is perfectly level, like a
calm sea."

"PERSONALITY 4 — UNDEFINED: VERTICAL. Arm straight up. The x-value never
changes, the equation is just x = c, and the slope can't even be
computed."

💡 [Explain WHY undefined exists — this is the conceptual key]:
"Why is a vertical line's slope 'undefined' rather than just 'really
big'? Because slope is rise OVER run — and for a vertical line, the run
is ZERO. You'd be dividing by zero, which math forbids. It's not a huge
number; it's a question with no answer. That's the difference between
zero slope and undefined slope."

✋ [Use the Socratic prompt on the slide:]
"I'll describe a scene; you call out the slope type. A ski slope going
straight down a mountain face? [negative] A flat road across a salt
flat? [zero] A vertical cliff? [undefined] A wheelchair ramp rising
gently? [positive]."

⚠️ [REGENTS TRAP — say this with emphasis, it appears on every exam]:
"Write this down in capital letters. ZERO slope is NOT the same as NO
slope. ZERO slope means the line is HORIZONTAL — flat. NO slope, or
UNDEFINED slope, means the line is VERTICAL. They are complete opposites,
and the Regents loves to test exactly this confusion. Horizontal = zero.
Vertical = undefined."

SLIDE 7 — DECODING y = mx + b 🔓
[Duration: ~7 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the graph of y = 2x + 1 with slope and y-intercept marked.
This is the heart of the lesson. Build the graph live, step by step, and
do NOT rush.]

✋ [Open with the Socratic prompt:]
"Look at the equation y = 2x + 1. Before we graph ANYTHING — predict.
Will this line go uphill or downhill? Where will it cross the y-axis?
Tell your neighbor your prediction."
   [Expected: uphill because slope 2 is positive; crosses y-axis at 1.]

"Beautiful. Now let's prove your prediction with a recipe. Think of
y = mx + b like a cooking recipe with two ingredients:
   b tells you where to START.
   m tells you how to MOVE from there."

[Now build the graph on the board or point to the diagram, narrating.]
"Step 1 — plot the y-intercept. Here b = 1, so I put my first dot at the
point (0, 1). That's home base.

Step 2 — use the slope to find the next point. Here m = 2, which I read
as the fraction 2 over 1: rise 2, run 1. So from (0, 1), I go UP 2 and
RIGHT 1, landing on (1, 3). Plot it.

Step 3 — draw a straight line through both points, and ALWAYS add arrows
on both ends to show it continues forever."

[Point to the formula box: "Start at b, move by m."]

💡 [DEEP CONCEPT — the most important idea on this slide]:
"Here's the elegance. Every linear equation in the world can be rewritten
into this one form, y = mx + b, and the moment it's in this form, the
graph is basically free — you just read b for where to start and m for
how to move. This is why we forced the Do Now into this form earlier.
Slope-intercept form isn't just one way to write a line; it's the form
that makes a line GRAPHABLE on sight."

⚠️ [Quick trap to flag:]
"When the slope is a whole number like 2, students forget it's secretly
the fraction 2/1. And when slope is negative, like -3/4, the negative
means you go DOWN 3 and right 4. The negative belongs to the rise. Keep
the run positive and let the sign live on top."

✋ "Quick one: for y = -1/2 x + 4, where's your first dot, and which way
   do you move? Fifteen seconds."
   [Reveal: start at (0, 4); from there go DOWN 1, RIGHT 2.]

SLIDE 8 — VIDEO 2: GRAPHING y = mx + b STEP BY STEP 🎬
[Duration: ~5 minutes — video + pair discussion]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Two clips linked: a 3-minute and a 4-minute walkthrough. Play one.
Prime students with a single focus question first.]

"Let's watch someone graph a line from scratch using the exact recipe we
just learned. Watch for ONE thing: notice the EXACT order they do it —
do they plot the y-intercept first, or use the slope first? Order
matters."

[Play the video — approximately 3 minutes.]

[After the video]

"Turn to a neighbor and finish this sentence in your own words, 60
seconds: 'To graph any line, I first ___, and then I ___.'"

✋ [Circulate. Expected: 'first plot the y-intercept (0, b), then use the
   slope rise-over-run to step to the next point.' Affirm variations.]

💡 [Use the Socratic prompt on the slide — this is a subtle, rich idea]:
"Here's a thinker. A slope of 3/4 can also be written as 6/8. If you
graph using 3/4 versus 6/8, do you get the same line? [Yes — exactly the
same line.] Why? Because equivalent fractions describe the same RATE of
change. Going up 3 and right 4 lands you on the same line as going up 6
and right 8 — you just took a bigger step. The slope is the relationship,
not the specific numbers. That's a deep idea worth holding onto."

SLIDE 9 — REAL WORLD: THE NYC TAXI RIDE 🚕
[Duration: ~7 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the scenario card, the equation, and the cost-vs-miles graph.
This is the payoff slide — connect everything back to the taxi question
from the roadmap.]

"Remember that taxi question from the start of class? Here's the real
math. A NYC taxi charges a $3.00 base fare, plus $2.50 for every mile
you ride. Let's turn that English sentence into algebra."

✋ [Use the Socratic prompt BEFORE giving the equation:]
"Before we write anything — what does the $3.00 represent on a graph?
What does the $2.50 represent? Which one is the slope, and which is the
y-intercept? Discuss for 30 seconds."
   [Expected: $3.00 = y-intercept (the cost before you move an inch);
   $2.50 = slope (the rate, cost per mile).]

"Exactly. Let's build it:
   y = total cost,  x = miles traveled
   slope m = 2.50  (the per-mile rate — this is rate of change)
   y-intercept b = 3.00  (the base fare, what you pay when x = 0)
So the equation is:  y = 2.50x + 3.00."

[Point to the graph. Show the y-intercept at $3 and the 5-mile point at
$15.50.]
"On the graph, the line doesn't start at zero — it starts at $3, because
the meter charges you the moment you get in. That $3 is b. Then it climbs
$2.50 for every mile — that steady climb is m."

✋ [Have students use the work-box on the slide:]
"Your turn. Compute the cost of a 5-mile ride and a 10-mile ride. Show
your work. 90 seconds."
   [Reveal: 5 miles → 2.50(5) + 3 = $15.50. 10 miles → 2.50(10) + 3 =
   $28.00. Confirm the 5-mile point matches the dot on the graph.]

💡 [DEEP CONCEPT]:
"Notice what 'slope' MEANS here — it's not just steepness anymore, it's
literally the price per mile. And the y-intercept isn't just 'where it
crosses' — it's the flat fee you pay before anything happens. This is the
whole reason linear functions matter: m is always a RATE, and b is always
a STARTING AMOUNT. Once you see that, you can model phone bills, gym
memberships, savings accounts — anything with a flat fee plus a steady
rate."

SLIDE 10 — DESMOS EXPLORATION 💻
[Duration: ~6 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[A short Desmos how-to video is linked. Then students explore live. Make
sure devices are open to desmos.com/calculator.]

"Now you get to play with a line and watch it move in real time. Go to
desmos.com/calculator and type exactly:  y = mx + b. Desmos will ask to
'add sliders' for m and b — click yes. Now you can drag m and b and watch
the line respond instantly."

[Give 2 minutes of free exploration. Circulate.]

✋ [Use the Socratic prompts on the slide as you circulate:]
"As you drag m from 1 up to 5 — what changes about the line? [It gets
steeper.] Now hold m still and drag b from 0 to 3 — what changes? [The
line slides up; its steepness stays the same.] So m controls steepness,
b controls position. Confirm it with your own eyes."

[Display the prediction questions.]
"Predict before you test:
  - What happens when m = 0? [The line goes flat — horizontal.]
  - Can m be a decimal like 0.5? [Yes — a gentle half-step slope.]
  - What do two parallel lines have in common? [Same slope, different b.]"

💡 [KEY INSIGHT — point to the card]:
"Here's a Regents gem you just discovered by dragging sliders. PARALLEL
lines have the SAME slope but different y-intercepts — they climb
identically and never meet. PERPENDICULAR lines have slopes that multiply
to -1 — they're negative reciprocals, like 2 and -1/2. You'll need both
of these facts on the exam, and now you've SEEN why they're true, not
just memorized them."

APPENDIX A — COMMON PITFALLS & HOW TO HANDLE THEM
================================================================================

PITFALL: Student divides only one term by the coefficient when solving for y.
  Handle: "Watch the Do Now move — from 3y = -2x + 6, you must divide
          EVERY term by 3, not just the y-term. That gives y = (-2/3)x + 2.
          A common slip is writing y = -2x + 2. Divide all three pieces or
          the equation is no longer true."

PITFALL: Student confuses rise and run (uses run over rise).
  Handle: "Rise comes BEFORE run, just like y comes before x in (x, y)...
          wait — that's the catch. In the slope FRACTION, rise (the change
          in y) goes on TOP, run (the change in x) on the bottom. m =
          Δy/Δx. Vertical change over horizontal change. Always."

PITFALL: Student says a vertical line has 'zero' slope.
  Handle: "This is THE Regents trap. A vertical line has a run of zero, so
          you'd divide by zero — that's UNDEFINED, not zero. ZERO slope is
          a flat, HORIZONTAL line. Horizontal = zero. Vertical = undefined.
          Opposites."

PITFALL: Student forgets the negative sign belongs to the rise.
  Handle: "For a slope of -3/4, keep the run positive and put the negative
          on the rise: go DOWN 3, RIGHT 4. Don't go down 3 AND left 4 —
          that double-counts the negative and gives you the wrong line."

PITFALL: Student plots the slope before the y-intercept.
  Handle: "Order matters. ALWAYS plant your first dot at the y-intercept
          (0, b) — that's your anchor. THEN use rise-over-run to step to
          the next point. If you start stepping before you've anchored,
          you've got nothing to step FROM."

PITFALL: Student treats a whole-number slope as if it has no run.
  Handle: "A slope of 2 is secretly 2/1. Rise 2, run 1. Every whole-number
          slope has an invisible denominator of 1. Write it as a fraction
          and the rise/run steps become obvious."

PITFALL: Student picks the wrong sign for slope in a word problem.
  Handle: "The word 'per' tells you it's a rate, but the CONTEXT tells you
          the sign. If the quantity is GROWING, slope is positive. If it's
          SHRINKING — a candle burning, a battery draining — slope is
          negative. Always ask: growing or shrinking?"

APPENDIX B — EXTENSION QUESTIONS FOR CURIOUS MINDS
================================================================================

1. Why does slope-intercept form make graphing so easy compared to
   standard form (Ax + By = C)?
   Answer: Because y = mx + b puts the two things you need to draw a line
   — a starting point and a direction — right out in the open. The b is a
   ready-to-plot point, (0, b), and m is a ready-to-use instruction,
   rise/run. Standard form hides both; you have to do algebra first to
   reveal them. Same line, but slope-intercept form is the line wearing a
   name tag.

2. Why do perpendicular lines have slopes that multiply to -1?
   Answer: When you rotate a line 90 degrees, its rise and run swap places
   AND one of them flips sign. A slope of 2 (rise 2, run 1) becomes
   -1/2 (rise -1, run 2) when turned a right angle. Multiply them: 2 ×
   (-1/2) = -1. That product of -1 is the algebraic fingerprint of a
   perfect right angle, which is why the Regents uses it to test
   perpendicularity.

3. How can two different equations describe the exact same line?
   Answer: Because you can multiply or rearrange an equation without
   changing its solutions. y = 2x + 1, 2y = 4x + 2, and -2x + y = 1 all
   graph identically. The line is the set of points that make the
   statement true, and scaling both sides equally never changes which
   points qualify. This also explains why slope 3/4 and slope 6/8 draw the
   same line — equivalent forms, identical truth.

4. Is every straight line a 'function'?
   Answer: Almost — but not the vertical one. A function requires that
   each x-value have exactly ONE y-value. A vertical line, x = c, has
   infinitely many y-values stacked on a single x, so it fails the
   vertical-line test and is NOT a function. That's a second reason
   vertical lines are the 'odd one out': undefined slope AND not a
   function. Every other line you graph today is a genuine linear
   function.

5. What does the y-intercept mean when x = 0 is impossible in real life?
   Answer: Sometimes b is a real starting value (the $3 base fare you pay
   before driving), and sometimes it's a mathematical anchor that the real
   situation never actually reaches. For a candle, x = 0 means "zero
   hours," which is real. But for, say, a person's height versus age,
   x = 0 (age zero) might give a b that's biologically meaningless. The
   intercept always pins the line to the y-axis mathematically — but
   whether it has a real-world meaning depends on whether x = 0 makes
   sense in the story.
