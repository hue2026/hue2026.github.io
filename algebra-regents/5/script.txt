[Display the Do Now slide with the three identification problems on

"Open your notebook. Three quick warm-up tasks. No talking yet — I
want your honest first instinct."

"PROBLEM 1: I show you 7x. How many terms does it have? What kind of
polynomial is that?"

"PROBLEM 2: I show you 3x + 5. How many terms? And what is its degree?"

"PROBLEM 3: x² + 4x + 1. Name every coefficient you can find."

[Give students 3 minutes. Then walk through the vocabulary cards.]

💡 [DEEP EXPLANATION — the four words that unlock everything]:
"Let me lock in four words, because the Regents uses them constantly
and assumes you know them cold.

A POLYNOMIAL is a sum of monomials — variables raised to whole-number
exponents, with coefficients. The whole-number part matters: x² is a
polynomial, but x raised to one-half, a square root, is not.

A COEFFICIENT is the number multiplying the variable. In 3x, the
coefficient is 3.

The DEGREE is the highest exponent in the polynomial. In x³ + 4x + 1,
the degree is 3 — we'd call that a cubic.

STANDARD FORM means terms written from highest degree down to lowest.
This is how the Regents wants every answer written. Make it a reflex."

✋ [Point to the Socratic question:] "In 5x² + 2x + 8, which number
   tells you the degree, and how can you tell at a glance?"
   [Expected: the exponent 2 on the leading term — degree 2, a
   quadratic. Affirm that the degree lives in the EXPONENT, never the
   coefficient.]

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

"Look at the slide. We've taken the polynomial 4x³ + 2x² + 7x + 3 and

"The leading term is 4x³. Its coefficient is 4. Its degree is 3 — it's

💡 [WHY THE NAMES MATTER]:
"We classify polynomials by how many terms they have. One term: a
MONOMIAL, like 5x. Two terms: a BINOMIAL, like 3x + 7. Three terms:
a TRINOMIAL, like x² + x + 1. These aren't just fancy words — 'binomial'
is exactly what FOIL is built for later in this lesson. The vocabulary
is a preview of the technique."

✋ [Point to the Socratic question:] "Look at 4x³ + 2x² + 7x + 3. What
   is the degree? And what is the coefficient of the x-term?"
   [Expected: degree 3; coefficient of x is 7. Watch for students who
   say the coefficient is 7x — gently correct: the coefficient is just
   the number, 7.]

SLIDE 6 — ADDING POLYNOMIALS: THE GOLDEN RULE
[Duration: ~6 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the adding slide with the three-step worked example on the
left and the "like terms" fruit analogy on the right.]

"Now we add. And there is exactly ONE golden rule, written at the top
of the slide: you can only combine LIKE TERMS. Let me show you, then
tell you why."

[Walk through the worked example: (3x² + 2x + 1) + (x² + 5x + 4).]

"Step 1: Drop the parentheses. Because this is addition, nothing
changes signs — every term keeps its sign. We now have
3x² + 2x + 1 + x² + 5x + 4.

Step 2: Group like terms by degree. The slide color-codes them. The
x²-terms together: 3x² and x². The x-terms together: 2x and 5x. The
constants together: 1 and 4.

Step 3: Combine. 3x² + x² is 4x². 2x + 5x is 7x. 1 + 4 is 5. The
answer, in standard form: 4x² + 7x + 5."

💡 [WHY LIKE TERMS — the fruit analogy made rigorous]:
"Why can't we combine 3x² with 2x? The slide says think of them like
fruit — x²-terms are apples, x-terms are oranges. You can add three
apples to one apple and get four apples. But three apples plus two
oranges? That's just three apples and two oranges. There is no single
word for it.

Here's the real reason underneath the metaphor: x² and x are genuinely
different quantities. If x is 10, then x² is 100 — a hundred times
larger than the x-term's contribution per unit. Combining them would
be like adding a length to an area. They live in different dimensions.
That's why x + x² stays x + x² — two different degrees, forever
separate."

[Point to the Regents Tip card:] "Always write your final answer in
STANDARD FORM — highest degree first. The Regents may mark you down
for a correct-but-disordered answer."

✋ [Socratic question:] "What happens if NONE of the terms are alike?
   Can you still 'add' the polynomials?" [Expected: yes — you just
   drop parentheses and rewrite; nothing combines. The sum is simply
   all the terms listed in standard form. 'Adding' doesn't require
   combining.]

✋ [Discuss:]
"One: the COLUMN METHOD — lining up like terms vertically, like

💡 [SYNTHESIS]:
"Notice he showed two methods: horizontal — left to right with

keeps YOU from making errors. Knowing both is a Regents superpower."

✋ [Socratic prompt:] "Could you solve the same problem a different way?
   What are the trade-offs?" [Let students articulate: speed vs.

SLIDE 8 — SUBTRACTING POLYNOMIALS: THE SIGN TRAP
[Duration: ~7 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the subtraction slide. The "Common Mistake" trap card is on
the left, the "Correct Method" beside it, the "Flip Every Sign" trick
on the right. Slow WAY down here — this is the highest-value slide in
the entire lesson.]

"This slide is the most important five minutes of today. The single
most common mistake on the polynomial section of the Regents lives
right here. I'm going to show you the wrong way first — on purpose —
because I want you to feel the trap before you fall into it."

[Point to the Common Mistake card: (5x² + 3x) − (2x² + x − 4).]

"Here's what almost everyone does the first time. They see the minus
sign, change ONLY the first term of the second group, and write
3x² + 3x − ... and then they freeze, or they leave the x and the
negative four untouched. That gives a WRONG answer, because the minus
sign in front of the parentheses doesn't belong to just the first
term — it belongs to the ENTIRE group."

💡 [THE DEEP WHY — distribute the negative one]:
"Here is the rule underneath the rule. A minus sign in front of a
parenthesis is secretly a negative one being multiplied through the
whole group. Watch:

  −(2x² + x − 4)  is really  −1 · (2x² + x − 4)

When you distribute that −1 to every single term, every sign flips.
The 2x² becomes −2x². The +x becomes −x. And here's the one everybody
misses — the −4 becomes POSITIVE 4. A negative times a negative is a
positive. That's where the +4 comes from."

[Walk through the Correct Method:]
"Step 1: Distribute the negative to ALL terms:
  5x² + 3x − 2x² − x + 4
Step 2: Combine like terms:
  5x² − 2x² = 3x², and 3x − x = 2x, and the +4 stands alone.
The correct answer: 3x² + 2x + 4."

[Point to the Memory Hook:] "Say this out loud, every single time you
subtract: 'Minus a group — flip the whole group.' I want to hear the
whole class say it. Ready — 'Minus a group, flip the whole group.'"

✋ [Socratic question — the conceptual capstone:] "Why does subtracting
   a negative become addition? Where does that rule come from?"
   [Expected insight: subtracting means 'removing,' and removing a debt
   is the same as gaining money — a number-line / opposite-of-opposite
   argument. Tie it back to the distributed −1.]

SLIDE 10 — MULTIPLYING: MONOMIAL × POLYNOMIAL
[Duration: ~6 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the distribution slide with the area-model boxes and the
worked example. Point to the colored boxes as you explain.]

"Now we multiply — and we build up in difficulty. We start with the
easiest case: one term times many. A monomial times a polynomial. The
tool is the distributive property you've used since seventh grade —
just dressed up."

[Walk through 3x(x² + 2x + 3):]
"The 3x out front has to multiply EVERY term inside. Look at the
area-model boxes — three boxes, because three terms inside, and the
3x stretches across all of them.
  3x · x² = 3x³
  3x · 2x = 6x²
  3x · 3 = 9x
The answer: 3x³ + 6x² + 9x."

💡 [THE EXPONENT RULE — and why it's ADD, not multiply]:
"When you multiplied 3x by x², where did the x³ come from? Here's the
rule on the slide: when you multiply powers of the same base, you ADD
the exponents. x¹ · x² = x³. Not x². ADD them.

Why add? Because exponents are shorthand for repeated multiplication.
x¹ means one x. x² means x · x. Put them together: x · (x · x) — that's
three x's multiplied, which is x³. You're counting how many x's are
multiplied in total, and counting means adding. This is Regents Trap
number three, and it catches people every year: x² · x³ is x⁵, never
x⁶."

[Point to the coefficient card:] "And don't forget — multiply the
COEFFICIENTS the normal way. 3x · 4x = 12x²: three times four is
twelve, x times x adds to x². Numbers multiply; exponents add."

✋ [Socratic question:] "Why do we ADD exponents when multiplying? What
   does x² · x³ really mean in fully expanded form?" [Expected:
   (x·x)·(x·x·x) = x⁵. Have a student write out all five x's on the
   board.]

SLIDE 11 — THE FOIL METHOD: BINOMIAL × BINOMIAL
[Duration: ~6 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the FOIL slide with the colored arc diagram. Trace each arc
with your finger as you name it — First, Outer, Inner, Last.]

"Now the star of the lesson: FOIL. This is the technique for
multiplying two binomials, and it appears on every single Regents
exam. FOIL is an acronym — First, Outer, Inner, Last — and it's just
a way to make sure every term meets every other term exactly once."

[Walk through (x + 3)(x + 2), tracing the colored arcs:]
"FIRST — the first term in each: x · x = x².
 OUTER — the two outermost: x · 2 = 2x.
 INNER — the two innermost: 3 · x = 3x.
 LAST — the last term in each: 3 · 2 = 6.
Now string them together: x² + 2x + 3x + 6. And here's the step
beginners forget — COMBINE the middle terms: 2x + 3x = 5x. Final
answer: x² + 5x + 6."

💡 [WHAT FOIL ACTUALLY IS — distribution in disguise]:
"Here's the secret the slide reveals: FOIL is not a new rule. It's the
distributive property, organized. Each term in the first binomial has
to multiply EVERY term in the second. The x in (x + 3) multiplies both
the x and the 2 — that's your First and Outer. The 3 multiplies both
the x and the 2 — that's your Inner and Last. FOIL is just a checklist
so you never miss one of the four products.

And that's WHY the middle terms almost always combine: the Outer and
Inner products are both x-terms, born from the same level of the
expression. 2x and 3x are like terms, so they merge into 5x. Every
time you FOIL two simple binomials, expect a three-term trinomial out
the other side."

[Point to the practice card:] "Now try (x − 4)(x + 1) on your own.
Watch the negative sign on that 4 — it travels with the term."
[Answer: x² − 3x − 4. Give 60 seconds.]

✋ [Socratic question:] "Is FOIL only for binomials? What would you do
   with (x + 1)(x² + x + 2)?" [Expected: FOIL is specifically for
   two-by-two; for bigger products you distribute each term — every
   term in the first meets every term in the second. FOIL is the
   special case, distribution is the general rule.]

[Duration: ~4 minutes]

on test day. These are FOIL results that follow such clean patterns
you can write the answer instantly — no four-step work needed."

"PATTERN 1 — Perfect Square: (a + b)² = a² + 2ab + b². For example,
(x + 3)² = x² + 6x + 9. Notice the middle term is exactly 2ab — two
times the product of the two pieces. The number-one mistake here:
students write (x + 3)² as x² + 9 and forget the middle term entirely.
A square of a binomial is NEVER just the two squares."

"PATTERN 2 — Difference of Squares: (a + b)(a − b) = a² − b². For
example, (x + 5)(x − 5) = x² − 25. The middle terms — the Outer and
Inner — are equal and opposite, so they cancel completely. +5x and
−5x annihilate each other. Two terms in, two terms out, nothing in
the middle."

💡 [WHY THESE MATTER]:
"These two patterns appear on EVERY Regents exam, and recognizing them
is pure speed. But here's the bigger payoff: these patterns run in
reverse for FACTORING — our very next unit. If you see x² − 25 next
week and instantly think 'difference of squares, (x + 5)(x − 5),'
you'll factor it in two seconds while your classmates grind. Learning
the forward pattern today is an investment in next week."

✋ [Socratic question:] "What happens to (a − b)²? Is it a² − b²?"

   classic trap; make the class verify with (x − 4)².]

APPENDIX A — COMMON PITFALLS & HOW TO HANDLE THEM
================================================================================

PITFALL: Student writes (x + 3)² = x² + 9.
  Handle: "Square means the thing times ITSELF — (x + 3)(x + 3). FOIL
          it and you get the middle term: x² + 6x + 9. The exponent
          doesn't distribute across a sum. Prove it to yourself with
          numbers: (2 + 3)² is 25, but 2² + 3² is only 13. They're
          not equal — so the middle term must exist."

PITFALL: Student combines 3x² + 2x into 5x² or 5x.
  Handle: "Different degrees can't combine — they're different
          quantities. If x = 10, then 3x² is 300 and 2x is 20. Their
          sum is 320, which is neither 5x² (500) nor 5x (50). They
          stay separate forever."

PITFALL: On subtraction, student flips only the first sign.
  Handle: "The minus sign is a hidden −1 multiplying the WHOLE group.
          Rewrite the problem literally as −1·(...) and distribute.
          Say the hook: 'Minus a group, flip the whole group.' Make
          it a verbal ritual until it's automatic."

PITFALL: Student multiplies exponents: x² · x³ = x⁶.
  Handle: "Write them out: (x·x)·(x·x·x). Count the x's — there are
          five. That's x⁵. Multiplying powers means COUNTING how many
          factors you have, and counting is adding. Multiply the
          coefficients, ADD the exponents."

PITFALL: Student leaves answer out of standard form.
  Handle: "Standard form is the Regents' expected format — highest
          degree first, descending. It's not optional politeness;
          it's how the scoring key is written. Make reordering the
          last step of every problem, every time."

PITFALL: Student forgets to combine the middle terms after FOIL.
  Handle: "After FOIL you have FOUR terms. The Outer and Inner are
          almost always like terms — same degree. Combining them is
          step five, and it's not optional. x² + 2x + 3x + 6 is only
          half-finished; x² + 5x + 6 is the answer."

APPENDIX B — EXTENSION QUESTIONS FOR ADVANCED STUDENTS
================================================================================

1. GENERALIZING FOIL: FOIL handles two-by-two. Write the full
   expansion of a binomial times a trinomial, (x + a)(x² + bx + c).
   How many products appear before combining? What's the pattern for
   (m terms)·(n terms)?

2. PASCAL'S TRIANGLE: Expand (x + 1)², (x + 1)³, and (x + 1)⁴. Look at
   the coefficients: 1-2-1, then 1-3-3-1, then 1-4-6-4-1. Where have
   you seen these numbers before? This is the Binomial Theorem in
   disguise.

3. DEGREE PREDICTION: When you multiply a degree-m polynomial by a
   degree-n polynomial, what's the degree of the product? Prove your
   rule. What about when you ADD them — is the degree predictable?

4. AREA & PERIMETER: A garden's area is x² + 8x + 15. Working
   backward, what could its length and width be? (This is factoring —
   a sneak peek at next unit.) Is the answer unique?

5. SPECIAL-PRODUCT PROOF: Prove algebraically that
   (a + b)(a − b) = a² − b² always, for ANY a and b, by full
   distribution. Then explain geometrically using a square with a
   smaller square cut from its corner.

APPENDIX C — QUICK REFERENCE: KEY RULES & DEFINITIONS
================================================================================

Like Terms:
  Combine ONLY terms with the same variable AND same exponent.
  3x² + 5x² = 8x²   ✓     3x² + 2x = 3x² + 2x   (cannot combine)

Adding Polynomials:
  Drop parentheses (signs unchanged) → group like terms → combine →
  write in standard form.

Subtracting Polynomials:
  Distribute the −1 to EVERY term of the second group (flip all
  signs) → then add. "Minus a group, flip the whole group."

Exponent Rule (multiplication, same base):
  xᵃ · xᵇ = xᵃ⁺ᵇ   (ADD exponents; MULTIPLY coefficients)

FOIL (binomial × binomial):
  (a + b)(c + d) = ac + ad + bc + bd
  First · Outer · Inner · Last → then combine like (middle) terms.

Special Products:
  (a + b)² = a² + 2ab + b²
  (a − b)² = a² − 2ab + b²
  (a + b)(a − b) = a² − b²   (difference of squares)

Standard Form:
  Terms ordered from highest degree to lowest. Always the final step.
