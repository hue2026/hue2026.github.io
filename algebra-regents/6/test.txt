"Good morning, algebra mathematicians! Welcome to Lesson 6. Last time
you learned to MULTIPLY — to take (x + 2)(x + 3) and build the
trinomial x² + 5x + 6. Today we do something that feels almost like
magic: we run that machine in reverse. We take the trinomial and
recover the two factors hiding inside it. That skill is called
FACTORING — and once you have it, you can SOLVE quadratic equations,
which is one of the most tested skills on the entire Algebra I Regents."

"Here is my promise to you. By the end of this hour, you will look at
an equation like x² + 5x + 6 = 0 and you will know exactly how to
break it apart and find the two values of x that make it true. No
guessing. No fear."

💡 [DEEP CONTEXT — deliver this slowly, with weight]:
"Here's the big idea that anchors everything today. Multiplying is
BUILDING — you snap pieces together into one expression. Factoring is
UN-BUILDING — taking that expression apart back into the pieces that
made it. Why would anyone want to un-build? Because of a strange and
beautiful property of the number zero. When something equals zero, its
factored form practically solves itself. We are going to turn one hard
equation into two easy ones, using nothing but the special power of
zero. Hold onto that — it's the engine of the whole lesson."

"Open your notebook. One task. List EVERY factor pair of 36. You have
90 seconds — go!"

[After 90 seconds, reveal the answer card.]

"Let's check. 1×36, 2×18, 3×12, 4×9, 6×6. And here's a pro move: you
can STOP at 6. Why? Because after 6, the pairs just repeat in reverse
— 9×4 is the same as 4×9. The moment your two factors cross over, you
have them all."

💡 [WHY THIS WARM-UP MATTERS — connect it forward]:
"This little game is not a warm-up to kill time. Listing factor pairs
is the EXACT skill that powers trinomial factoring later this hour.
When you factor x² + 7x + 10, you'll be hunting through the factor
pairs of 10 looking for the right one. The faster you can rattle off
factor pairs, the faster you factor. So get good at this now."

[Walk through the four vocabulary cards.]

💡 [THE WORDS THAT UNLOCK TODAY]:
"Four words the Regents assumes you know cold.

A POLYNOMIAL is an expression built of terms. A BINOMIAL has two terms
— like x + 5; a TRINOMIAL has three — like x² + 5x + 6. Think bicycle,
tricycle: 'bi' is two, 'tri' is three.

A FACTOR is a quantity that MULTIPLIES to build an expression. So
FACTORING means rewriting a sum as a PRODUCT — turning x² + 5x + 6
into (x + 2)(x + 3).

A QUADRATIC EQUATION has an x² term and is set equal to zero. Its
standard form is ax² + bx + c = 0.

And the ZERO PRODUCT PROPERTY — the star of the show — says that if a
product equals zero, one of its factors MUST be zero. We'll prove why
soon."

✋ [Point to the Socratic question:] "Which pair of factors of 36 has
   a SUM of 13? Which pair sums to 12?" [Expected: 4 and 9 sum to 13;
   nothing standard sums to 12 from whole pairs except... let them
   wrestle — the point is to make them HUNT by sum, not just by
   product. That hunt is the detective skill of Slide 6.]

SLIDE 4 — GCF FACTORING: UN-DISTRIBUTING
[Duration: ~6 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the area-model diagram showing 6x² + 9x = 3x(2x + 3). Point
to the boxes as you explain how 3x is shared across both.]

"We start factoring with the simplest, most important move of all:
pulling out the GREATEST COMMON FACTOR — the GCF. And here's the rule
you tattoo on your brain: GCF FIRST, ALWAYS. Before you try anything
fancy, check what every term shares."

[Walk through 6x² + 9x.]

"Look at 6x² + 9x. Two questions. First: what number divides into both
6 and 9? Three. Second: how many x's do BOTH terms own? The first term
has two x's, the second has one — so they share ONE x. Put those
together: the greatest common factor is 3x. We pull it out front and
write what's left inside: 3x(2x + 3)."

💡 [THE CLEVER WAY TO THINK ABOUT IT — the club membership fee]:
"Here's a picture that makes GCF click. Think of the GCF as a CLUB
MEMBERSHIP FEE — it's what every term had to pay to get in. Both 6x²
and 9x paid 3x to join. Factoring just collects that fee back out
front and shows you what each member has left after paying. 6x² paid
3x and has 2x left. 9x paid 3x and has 3 left. So: 3x(2x + 3).

And notice — this is exactly LAST lesson's distribution running
backward. Distributing 3x INTO (2x + 3) gives 6x² + 9x. Factoring
pulls it back OUT. They're inverse operations."

[Point to the Check Your Work card.]

💡 [YOUR ERASER-PROOF SAFETY NET]:
"Because factoring and distributing are inverses, you have a built-in
check on EVERY factoring problem. Distribute your answer back out. If
3x(2x + 3) gives you 6x² + 9x — the thing you started with — you're
correct, guaranteed. No quadratic factoring answer should ever go
unchecked. Multiply it back."

✋ [Point to the Socratic question:] "In 6x² + 9x, what's the biggest
   thing you could pull out of BOTH terms at once?" [Expected: 3x.
   Watch for students who pull only the 3 or only the x — push them to
   take the GREATEST common factor, both the number AND the variable.]

SLIDE 6 — FACTORING TRINOMIALS: THE TWO-NUMBER DETECTIVE
[Duration: ~7 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the X-diamond diagram for x² + 7x + 10. Point to the top
"MULTIPLY to c" and bottom "ADD to b" as you build the story.]

"Now the heart of the lesson: factoring trinomials of the form
x² + bx + c. And we're going to do it like detectives. Every trinomial
hides a PAIR of numbers, and your job is to catch them."

[Walk through x² + 7x + 10 with the X-diamond.]

"Here's the case. We need two numbers — call them the suspects — that
satisfy TWO alibis at once. They must MULTIPLY to c, which is 10. And
they must ADD to b, which is 7. So list the factor pairs of 10: 1 and
10, 2 and 5. Now scan for the pair that ALSO adds to 7. One plus ten
is eleven — no. Two plus five is seven — YES. Case closed. Drop them
into the parentheses: (x + 2)(x + 5)."

💡 [WHY TWO JOBS AT ONCE — the structure underneath]:
"Why must the pair multiply to c and add to b? Run the FOIL backward
from last lesson. When you multiply (x + 2)(x + 5), the LAST terms
multiply to give the constant — 2 times 5 is 10, that's your c. And
the OUTER plus INNER terms combine to give the middle — 2x plus 5x is
7x, that's your b. So the two numbers that built the trinomial are
exactly the pair that multiplies to c and adds to b. Factoring is just
reverse-engineering the FOIL you already know."

[Point to the Sign Detective Rules card — this is high-value.]

💡 [READING THE SIGNS — the detective's tell]:
"The signs tell you what kind of pair to hunt for. Two rules:

If c is POSITIVE, both signs MATCH each other — and they match the
sign of b. So x² + 7x + 10 gives (x + 2)(x + 5): both plus.

If c is NEGATIVE, the signs DIFFER — one plus, one minus — and the
bigger number takes the sign of b. So x² + 3x − 10 gives (x + 5)(x − 2):
the 5 is bigger, b is positive, so 5 gets the plus."

✋ [Point to the Socratic question:] "For x² + 7x + 10, you found 2 and
   5. What if the problem were x² + 11x + 10 — would your pair change?"
   [Expected: yes — now you need a pair multiplying to 10 that adds to
   11, which is 1 and 10. Same product, different sum, different
   factors. Drives home that BOTH jobs matter.]

SLIDE 7 — THE ZERO PRODUCT PROPERTY: THE POWER OF ZERO
[Duration: ~7 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the slide. Lead with the Socratic riddle on the left, then
the formula, then the worked example. Slow down — this is the
conceptual capstone of the lesson.]

"Now we connect factoring to SOLVING. Start with a riddle, on the
slide: I'm thinking of two numbers. Their product is exactly zero.
What can you tell me, with absolute certainty, about at least one of
my numbers?"

✋ [Let them answer.] "Right — at least one of them MUST be zero. Could
   BOTH be nonzero? No. Try it — name me two nonzero numbers that
   multiply to zero. You can't. It's impossible."

💡 [WHY ZERO IS SPECIAL — the property nothing else has]:
"Here's why zero has a superpower no other number has. Take any other
target — say, 12. There are endless ways to hit it: 3 times 4, 2 times
6, even 0.5 times 24. Knowing a product is 12 tells you almost nothing
about the factors. But ZERO is different. Zero is the one number you
CANNOT sneak up on by multiplying. 0.0001 times 0.0001 is tiny — but
it's still not zero. The ONLY way a product equals zero is if one of
the factors actually IS zero. That certainty is what turns one hard
equation into two easy ones."

[Point to the formula box: If A · B = 0, then A = 0 or B = 0. Then
walk the worked example x² + 2x − 15 = 0.]

"Watch it work. Solve x² + 2x − 15 = 0.
 Step 1: Is it set equal to zero? Yes — we're ready.
 Step 2: Detective pair for c = −15, b = 2. Since c is negative, signs
         differ. The pair 5 and −3: multiplies to −15, adds to +2. ✓
 Step 3: Factor: (x + 5)(x − 3) = 0.
 Step 4: Now zero's superpower fires — split it: x + 5 = 0  OR
         x − 3 = 0.
 Step 5: Solve each: x = −5 or x = 3."

[Point to the Check card.]
"And ALWAYS check both. Plug in −5: (−5)² + 2(−5) − 15 = 25 − 10 − 15
= 0. ✓ Plug in 3: 9 + 6 − 15 = 0. ✓ Both work."

[Point to the Regents Trap card — circle it, say it twice.]
💡 [THE #1 ZERO-PRODUCT TRAP]:
"The single biggest trap the Regents sets here: the property ONLY
works when the product equals ZERO. If you see (x + 2)(x − 5) = 4, you
may NOT write x + 2 = 4. That's wrong — 4 doesn't have zero's
superpower. You must FIRST multiply out, move everything to one side
so it equals zero, THEN factor and split. Set to zero first, every
single time."

✋ [Point to the Socratic question:] "Why did ONE equation give us TWO
   answers? What does that suggest about the SHAPE of the graph of
   y = x² + 2x − 15?" [Expected: it must cross the x-axis at two
   points. Tee up the parabola — we'll see it on Slide 9.]

SLIDE 9 — SEE IT ON THE GRAPH (DESMOS): ROOTS YOU CAN SEE
[Duration: ~6 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the parabola diagram for y = x² − 2x − 15, crossing the
x-axis at x = −3 and x = 5. If you have a projector, open Desmos live.]

"Everything we've done so far has been algebra on paper. Now let's SEE
it. Go to desmos.com/calculator and type y = x² − 2x − 15. A
parabola — a U-shaped curve — appears. Click the two gray points where
the curve crosses the x-axis."

"Look at where it crosses: x = −3 and x = 5. Those are the EXACT same
answers we'd get by factoring (x − 5)(x + 3) = 0. The algebra and the
picture agree."

💡 [WHY THE CROSSINGS ARE THE ANSWERS — y = 0 is the secret]:
"Here's the deep link. Solving x² − 2x − 15 = 0 means finding the
x-values that make y EQUAL ZERO. And on a graph, y = 0 is exactly the
x-axis — the horizontal line where height is zero. So the solutions of
the equation are precisely the spots where the parabola TOUCHES the
x-axis. Factoring finds them with algebra; the graph shows them with a
picture. Same truth, two languages."

[Point to the Key Insight card.]
💡 [FOUR NAMES, ONE IDEA]:
"This is huge for the Regents, because the test uses four different
words for the same thing: SOLUTIONS, ROOTS, ZEROS, and X-INTERCEPTS.
They all mean the identical idea — the x-values where the parabola
hits the x-axis. When a Regents question asks for the 'zeros' or the
'roots,' it's asking you to factor and solve. Don't let the vocabulary
fool you."

✋ [Point to the prediction questions:] "Without graphing — where will
   y = (x + 1)(x − 6) cross the x-axis? What equation has roots at
   x = 2 and x = −7? And can a parabola cross the x-axis only once?
   Never?" [Expected: crosses at −1 and 6; an equation like
   (x − 2)(x + 7) = 0; yes — a parabola can touch once (a double root)
   or never (no real roots). Great preview of next lesson.]

SLIDE 10 — REAL-WORLD APPLICATION: THE GARDEN PLOT
[Duration: ~6 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the garden problem with the rectangle diagram. This is where
abstract factoring becomes something students can picture.]

"Here's why any of this matters. A rectangular garden has an area of
54 square feet. Its length is 3 feet longer than its width. Find the
dimensions."

✋ [Slide's opening Socratic question:] "Before any algebra — if the
   width were 5, the length would be 8. Does 5 × 8 = 54? No, it's 40.
   So 5 is too small. How could a single equation search EVERY
   possibility at once, instead of us guessing?" [Let them feel the
   need for algebra.]

[Walk through the Build-the-Equation steps shown on the slide.]
"Let's let the algebra do the searching.
 Let width = w. Then length = w + 3.
 Area = length × width, so w(w + 3) = 54.
 Multiply out: w² + 3w = 54.
 Set to zero — remember, ALWAYS — w² + 3w − 54 = 0.
 Detective pair for c = −54, b = 3: signs differ, bigger takes the
 plus. The pair is 9 and −6. Factor: (w + 9)(w − 6) = 0.
 Split: w = −9 or w = 6."

[Point to the Reject Impossible Roots card — this is the Regents
maturity moment.]
💡 [WHY WE THROW ONE ANSWER AWAY]:
"Algebra honestly found TWO roots: w = −9 and w = 6. But stop and
think about what w MEANS — it's the width of a real garden. Can a
garden be NEGATIVE nine feet wide? Of course not. So we REJECT w = −9.
It's a perfectly valid algebraic solution that's physically
impossible. The width is 6 feet, and the length is 6 + 3 = 9 feet.
On every real-world Regents problem, after you solve, ask: does this
answer make sense in context? Lengths, times, and counts can't be
negative."

✋ [Socratic close:] "Why does the EQUATION produce an answer that
   reality rejects? What's the difference between a mathematically
   valid root and a physically meaningful one?" [Let students chew on
   the idea that the equation doesn't 'know' it's describing a garden.]
