🔑 Key Vocabulary
Polynomial · Binomial · Trinomial
Expressions made of terms. Bi = 2 terms (x + 5), tri = 3 terms (x² + 5x + 6) — like bicycle and tricycle!
Factor
A quantity that multiplies to build an expression. Factoring = rewriting a sum as a product.
Quadratic Equation · Zero Product Property
An equation with an x² term, set equal to 0. If a product equals zero, one of its factors must be zero.
ax² + bx + c = 0
standard form of a quadratic equation
Part 2 · GCF

Factoring = Un-Distributing 🔄

Look at 6x² + 9x. What number divides into both 6 and 9? How many x's do both terms "own"? What is the biggest thing you could pull out of both terms at once?
Clever way to think about it: the GCF is the "club membership fee" — it's what every term paid in. Factoring just collects the fee back out front and shows what each member has left.

6x² + 9x → both terms paid 3x → 3x(2x + 3)
Factor out the GCF FIRST — always!
🔍 Check Your Work
Distribute it back: 3x(2x + 3) = 6x² + 9x ✓ Factoring and distributing are inverse operations — your eraser-proof safety net.

Part 2 · Trinomials

The Two-Number Detective 🕵️

For x² + 7x + 10, list the factor pairs of 10. Which pair also adds to 7? What if the problem were x² + 11x + 10 — would your pair change?
The detective's method: the criminal pair must satisfy two alibis at once — multiply to c, add to b. List the factor pairs of c (your suspects), then scan for the one whose sum is b. Case closed: drop them into (x + __)(x + __).
x² + bx + c = (x + m)(x + n)
where m·n = c and m + n = b
⚠️ Sign Detective Rules
c positive → both signs match b (x² − 7x + 10 → (x−2)(x−5)). c negative → signs differ, bigger number takes b's sign (x² − 3x − 10 → (x−5)(x+2)).
Part 3 · Zero Product Property

The Power of Zero 🎯

I'm thinking of two numbers. Their product is exactly 0. What can you tell me — with absolute certainty — about at least one of my numbers? Could BOTH be nonzero? Why not?
Why zero is special: zero is the only number you can't "sneak up on" by multiplying. 0.0001 × 0.0001 is tiny, but it's still not zero. The only way a product is 0 is if a factor IS 0 — that certainty turns one hard equation into two easy ones!
If A · B = 0, then A = 0 or B = 0
⚠️ Regents Trap
The property ONLY works for zero! If (x−2)(x−5) = 4, you may NOT write x−2 = 4. First move everything to one side so the product equals 0.
Worked Example: x² − 2x − 15 = 0
1. Already equals 0? ✓ Standard form ✓
2. Detective pair for c = −15, b = −2: −5 and +3
3. Factor: (x − 5)(x + 3) = 0
4. Split: x − 5 = 0  or  x + 3 = 0
5. Solve: x = 5 or x = −3
✅ Check both! 5² − 2(5) − 15 = 25 − 10 − 15 = 0 ✓   (−3)² − 2(−3) − 15 = 9 + 6 − 15 = 0 ✓
Why did ONE equation give us TWO answers? What does that suggest about the shape of the graph of y = x² − 2x − 15?

Part 4 · Real World

🌱 The Garden Plot Problem

🗺️ The Scenario
A rectangular garden has an area of 54 ft². Its length is 3 ft longer than its width. What are the dimensions?
Before any algebra — if the width were 5, what would the length be? Does 5 × 8 = 54 work? How could an equation search every possibility at once?
Build the Equation:
• width = w    length = w + 3
• Area: w(w + 3) = 54
• Standard form: w² + 3w − 54 = 0
• Factor: (w + 9)(w − 6) = 0 → w = −9 or w = 6
width = 6 ft, length = 9 ft
⚠️ Reject Impossible Roots
Algebra found w = −9 too, but a garden can't be −9 ft wide! In real-world problems, always ask: does this answer make sense?

Try It: 2x² + 7x + 3 = 0
