## 🎌 SLIDE 1 — TITLE SLIDE
### *Storytelling Techniques in Anime*
### ⏱ Estimated Time: 3 minutes

---

`[As students enter, have Slide 1 displayed. Play Lo-Fi anime music quietly in the background if possible. Stand near the board, not behind a desk.]`

**"Good [morning/afternoon], everyone. Welcome back to Anime Club."**

**"Before I say anything else — I want you to close your eyes for just three seconds. Think about the last time an anime made you feel something really strong. Maybe your heart raced. Maybe you gasped. Maybe you actually had to stop yourself from immediately watching the next episode at midnight."**

`[Pause 3–4 seconds. Let it be quiet.]`

**"Okay. Open your eyes."**

**"Whatever that feeling was — that was not an accident. Today we are going to pull back the curtain and learn exactly how the people who make anime engineer that feeling. On purpose. Every single time."**

**"This lesson is called Storytelling Techniques in Anime. By the time we leave today, you will never watch anime the same way again — because you will see the machinery behind it."**

`[Gesture to the Big Question card on the slide:]`

❓ **"Here is today's big question — and I want you to hold it in your head all lesson: When you watch anime, what is it that makes you feel something — or need to see the next episode right now?"**

`[Do NOT answer it. Do not take responses yet. Let it sit.]`

**"We're going to come back to that question multiple times today. Keep it with you."**

---

> 💡 **PhD Note:** The question intentionally targets two separate phenomena — emotional engagement and compulsive continuation. These map to distinct academic concepts: the first relates to *pathos* and narrative identification theory (Murray Smith's "character alignment," 1995); the second relates to the *Zeigarnik Effect* (Bluma Zeigarnik, 1927) — which we will introduce explicitly on Slide 7. Knowing both lets you respond with sophistication if a student accidentally articulates one of them early.

## ✏️ SLIDE 2 — DO NOW: Demon Slayer Clip
### *Watch & Notice*
### ⏱ Estimated Time: 7 minutes (2 min watch, 2 min write, 3 min discuss)

---

`[Advance to Slide 2. Click the thumbnail to launch the Demon Slayer clip. Make sure the volume is audible to the whole room.]`

**"Before I explain anything — I want you to just watch. Do not try to analyze yet. Just feel it."**

`[Play the clip. Watch students' faces, not the screen. Note who reacts, who leans in, who looks confused.]`

`[After the clip ends:]`

**"Okay. Notebooks out. You have exactly 90 seconds. I want you to write down three things from those three prompts on the board:"**

**"One — what storytelling elements did you notice? Think music, camera angle, how fast or slow the pacing felt."**
**"Two — how did it make you feel? And more importantly, what specifically caused that feeling?"**
**"Three — if you had to name one technique the director used, what would you call it?"**

`[Set a 90-second timer. Walk around and glance at notebooks — do not hover. Note interesting phrases students write.]`

`[After timer:]`

**"Turn and share with the person next to you. You have 45 seconds each."**

`[After partner share:]`

❓ **"Who noticed something their partner noticed too?"**
`[Take 2–3 hands. Affirm specifics: "The music choice — yes, tell me more about that."]`

❓ **"Did anyone name a technique? What did you call it?"**
`[Students may say "sad music," "memories," "slow motion." Do not correct vocabulary yet — that's for Slide 4. Instead say:]`

**"Interesting. Hold that word. We are going to give it a more precise name in a few minutes."**

> 💡 **PhD Note:** The scene depicted — Tanjiro discovering his family slaughtered and Nezuko transformed — is one of anime's most studied "inciting incidents." Scholar Thomas Lamarre (in *The Anime Machine*, 2009) specifically discusses how Demon Slayer director Haruo Sotozaki uses what he calls "affective animation" — facial micro-expressions held for 2–3 extra frames beyond Western animation norms to force emotional identification. The slow dolly-in camera move in this scene is directly borrowed from Akira Kurosawa's live-action filmmaking vocabulary. This cross-media lineage — manga → live-action cinema → anime — is uniquely Japanese.

> 🔁 **If students are stuck:** "Let me ask it differently. When Tanjiro finds his family, the music gets very quiet before it gets loud. Why do you think the director made that choice?"

## 🗝️ SLIDE 4 — VOCABULARY
### *5 Key Terms*
### ⏱ Estimated Time: 8 minutes

---

`[Advance to Slide 4.]`

**"These five terms are the tools of the trade. Professional anime writers, manga authors, and directors use these words in production meetings. If you walked into Studio Ghibli's story room right now, you would hear these words."**

**"Let's go through each one — and for every term, I want you to try to connect it to an anime you've actually seen before I explain it."**

`[Walk through the five cards:]`

---

### 📈 Narrative Arc

**"Narrative arc. The word 'arc' comes from architecture — it's a curved line that rises, reaches a peak, and comes back down. In storytelling, it means the shape of a story from start to finish."**

**"Every complete story has a beginning, rising action — where things get harder and more complicated — a climax, which is the peak moment of highest tension or change, falling action, and resolution."**

**"In Naruto, think about the Chunin Exams — that's one complete arc inside a much longer series. It has its own beginning, its own climax when Naruto fights Neji, and its own resolution. Within that arc, there's a smaller arc for every fight."**

❓ **"Can anyone think of an arc from an anime they've watched — even if they don't call it an arc? What was the big 'final moment' in that story section?"**

---

### ⏪ Flashback

**"Flashback. A scene that jumps backward in time. The story pauses the present action and shows us something from the past."**

**"Flashbacks in anime are not just memories. They are a deliberate choice about what information the audience needs, and exactly when they need it for maximum emotional impact."**

**"In Demon Slayer, Tanjiro's flashbacks to his father Tanjuro are placed at the exact moments when Tanjiro is closest to death. That is not random — the writer is telling us: 'The past is the reason he doesn't give up in the present.'"**

❓ **"Why would a writer wait to reveal important information? Why not just tell us at the beginning?"**

`[Take 2–3 responses. Look for someone who articulates "suspense" or "we need to understand Tanjiro before we can understand the flashback." Affirm and elaborate:]`

**"Exactly — a flashback only has emotional weight if we already care about the character. If you don't know Tanjiro yet, seeing his father means nothing. Timing is everything."**

---

### 👁️ Foreshadowing

**"Foreshadowing. Planting a small detail, image, or line of dialogue early in a story that will become much more important later."**

**"The word comes from 'shadow' — a shadow appears before the object that casts it. Foreshadowing is the shadow of something that hasn't happened yet."**

**"Attack on Titan is the most foreshadowing-dense anime ever made. The creator, Hajime Isayama, said in interviews that he wrote the ending first and then built Season 1 to contain hundreds of hidden clues. Fans rewatched the series and found over 200 foreshadowing moments — some hidden in background images, some in single lines of dialogue that seemed unimportant at the time."**

❓ **"Here's a harder question: Can foreshadowing fail? What would make a viewer feel cheated rather than satisfied when the payoff arrives?"**

`[Students may say "if it's too obvious" or "if it doesn't make sense." Affirm both:]`

**"Exactly. The rule is: foreshadowing must be invisible the first time, and inevitable the second time. You shouldn't see it coming — but looking back, it should feel like it could not have happened any other way."**

---

### ⚡ Cliffhanger

**"Cliffhanger. Ending a scene or episode at the moment of highest tension — before the resolution. The audience is left literally hanging off a cliff."**

**"The term comes from 19th-century serialized novels, where authors like Charles Dickens would end chapters at crisis moments to force readers to buy the next issue. Anime inherited this technique and took it to an extreme."**

**"Think about the last time you watched a show and physically could not stop. 'Just one more episode.' That feeling is a cliffhanger working exactly as designed."**

❓ **"Is a cliffhanger honest storytelling — or is it manipulation? Defend your answer."**

`[This is intentionally provocative. Both answers are correct depending on perspective. Look for nuance.]`

**"Both are true, actually — and that tension is part of what makes storytelling an art and a craft. The technique is a tool. What matters is how it's used."**

---

### 🌱 Character Development

**"Character development. How a character changes over time — in their beliefs, their abilities, their understanding of themselves or the world — as a direct result of the challenges they face."**

**"The key word is 'direct result.' A character who changes without any cause isn't developing — they're just inconsistent. Real character development means we can trace every change back to a specific moment of struggle or choice."**

**"Izuku Midoriya in My Hero Academia starts as a boy with no powers who cries watching heroes on TV. By Season 6, he's nearly destroying his own body to protect others. That change didn't happen overnight — it happened across hundreds of moments of failure, pain, and conscious choice."**

❓ **"What is the difference between a character who changes and a character who develops? Can something change without developing?"**

`[Brilliant question to sit with — students often can't articulate it right away. A rock changes when you break it; it doesn't develop. Development implies intentionality and growth from the inside.]`

---

> 💡 **PhD Note:** The five terms map to academic narratology: Narrative Arc = Freytag's Pyramid (Gustav Freytag, 1863, updated for anime through Osamu Tezuka's manga grammar); Flashback = *analepsis* in Gérard Genette's narrative theory (*Narrative Discourse*, 1972); Foreshadowing = *prolepsis* or narrative prolepsis; Cliffhanger = the Aristotelian concept of *peripeteia* (reversal of fortune) applied to serial structure; Character Development = Aristotle's *anagnorisis* (recognition/discovery) driving *hamartia* (tragic flaw) toward *catharsis*. The fact that Greek dramatic concepts from 400 BCE still apply to a 21st-century Japanese medium is itself worth mentioning to curious students.

## ⏪ SLIDE 5 — TECHNIQUE SPOTLIGHT: FLASHBACK
### *Demon Slayer · Mugen Train Arc*
### ⏱ Estimated Time: 6 minutes

---

`[Advance to Slide 5. Click the thumbnail to play the Demon Slayer Tanjiro's Father clip.]`

**"Watch this clip specifically for the flashback technique. Notice: when does the scene cut to the past? What is happening in the present at that moment? And how does the memory change what Tanjiro does next?"**

`[Play clip. After it ends:]`

**"So. What did you notice?"**

`[Take 2–3 responses. Then guide deeper:]`

**"Let me add some context. This is from the Mugen Train arc — Tanjiro is literally inside a dream designed to trap him. He is as close to death as he has ever been. And what does his mind go to? His father."**

**"Tanjuro Kamado — Tanjiro's father — was never shown to be a demon slayer. He was described as a sickly man who could barely leave his bed. But this flashback reveals that he danced the Hinokami Kagura — a ceremonial fire dance — every New Year's Eve, all night, in the snow, without getting cold. That impossibility is the clue that there is more to the Kamado family bloodline than we understood."**

**"The writer, Koyoharu Gotouge, had planned this reveal since Chapter 1. It is foreshadowing AND a flashback operating at the same time."**

❓ **"What would have been lost if this scene had happened in Episode 1 — before we knew Tanjiro at all?"**

`[Let students think. Then:]`

❓ **"Here's a harder version of that question: Why do you think the director chose a dream for this flashback, rather than just a regular memory?"**

`[Look for: "Because in a dream, you can't control it — it feels like it's coming from somewhere deeper than just his head." If a student says this, stop and praise it explicitly.]`

**"That answer is PhD-level thinking. A dream flashback implies the unconscious — it suggests that this memory lives in Tanjiro's body, not just his brain. That is exactly what the director intended."**

> 💡 **PhD Note:** In Jungian psychological terms used widely in anime scholarship, the dream-flashback in Mugen Train functions as a *visitation from the collective unconscious* — Tanjuro appears as an archetypal guide figure, similar to how Joseph Campbell's *monomyth* (Hero's Journey) describes the "supernatural aid" stage. Isayama and Gotouge both studied manga construction under editors who were explicitly trained in Tezuka's narrative methodology, which itself drew from American and European film grammar. The cross-cultural DNA of anime storytelling is uniquely layered.

> 🔁 **If students are stuck:** "Let me ask it this way — Tanjiro didn't choose to have this memory. It came to him. What does that tell us about where the memory comes from?"

## 👁️ SLIDE 6 — TECHNIQUE SPOTLIGHT: FORESHADOWING
### *Attack on Titan*
### ⏱ Estimated Time: 6 minutes

---

`[Advance to Slide 6. Play the AoT Foreshadowing clip.]`

**"This video was made by a fan who rewatched Attack on Titan after seeing the ending and catalogued every foreshadowing moment they could find. Watch it and count how many times you think 'wait, that was there the whole time?'"**

`[Play clip. After:]`

**"Before I say anything — what hit you hardest?"**

`[Take responses. Then add:]`

**"Hajime Isayama said in a 2021 interview that when he submitted the first chapter to his editor in 2009, he already had the ending written. He knew who Eren really was. He knew what the Titans were. He knew the truth about the walls."**

**"That means every single episode of Season 1, which most audiences watched as a pure horror-action series, was actually a puzzle box — and we didn't even know we were holding the box."**

**"The key to foreshadowing is that it has to work on TWO levels simultaneously: the surface level, where it seems unimportant or confusing, and the deep level, where it is the answer to the biggest mystery."**

❓ **"Can foreshadowing be too obvious? What is the difference between a clue and a spoiler?"**

`[Classic Socratic tension. Let students argue:]`

❓ **"Follow-up: Is it possible for foreshadowing to be so subtle that it doesn't count? If no one ever notices it, did it work?"**

`[This is a genuine academic debate in narratology. There is no right answer. Encourage the disagreement.]`

**"Here's the writing application: When you design your own anime character's story, every important reveal needs to have at least one foreshadowing seed planted early. Not an obvious one — a small one. A detail that seems like texture but is actually a key."**

❓ **"Right now — think about your character. Is there something about their backstory that a reader shouldn't know yet? Where could you hide a tiny clue to that secret in the opening scene?"**

> 💡 **PhD Note:** In anime scholarship, AoT's foreshadowing is studied as an example of *narrative architecture* — the term coined by scholar Marc Steinberg in *Anime's Media Mix* (2012). Isayama's approach mirrors what Umberto Eco called *open texts* — narratives that reward multiple readings at different levels of meaning. The fact that AoT's foreshadowing is visible on rewatch but not on first viewing is the defining feature of the technique when done at the highest level. The contrast with weaker foreshadowing (often called "telegraphing") is that telegraphing only works on one level — it's obvious on first viewing.

## ⚡ SLIDE 7 — TECHNIQUE SPOTLIGHT: CLIFFHANGER
### ⏱ Estimated Time: 6 minutes

---

`[Advance to Slide 7. Play the Top 10 Anime Cliffhangers clip.]`

**"Watch this with one specific question in mind: what is happening in the story at the exact moment each episode cuts to black?"**

`[Play clip. After:]`

**"Every single one of those cuts happened at the same structural moment: after something enormous changes — but before we know the consequence. That is the anatomy of a cliffhanger."**

**"Now I want to teach you a word: the Zeigarnik Effect. Bluma Zeigarnik was a Soviet psychologist working in the 1920s. She noticed that waiters could remember every order a table hadn't been served yet — but forgot the orders the moment the food arrived."**

**"Her conclusion: human brains remember unfinished tasks more powerfully than completed ones. We are literally wired to be haunted by things left unresolved."**

**"Anime writers know this. When an episode ends on a cliffhanger, they are hacking your brain's operating system."**

❓ **"Is that manipulation — or is it the writer doing their job? What's the difference?"**

`[Let students debate. Then:]`

❓ **"Here's a different question: Can a cliffhanger fail? Have you ever watched a show that tried to end on a cliffhanger and you just... didn't care? What was missing?"**

`[This is the deeper question — what makes a cliffhanger work is not the tension itself, but how much the audience cares about the characters. A cliffhanger with characters we don't care about is just a slow episode ending.]`

**"That is the most important thing about cliffhangers: they only work if the audience is already emotionally invested. You can't manufacture suspense with technique alone. The technique amplifies feeling — it doesn't create it from nothing."**

**"So in your own storyboard today — a cliffhanger is not just 'something bad happens at the end.' A cliffhanger is 'we know something terrible is about to happen to a character we deeply care about — and we don't get to find out the result yet.'"**

> 💡 **PhD Note:** The cliffhanger as a structural device was formalized in 19th-century serial fiction (Dickens, Collins) and then absorbed into early cinema through "chapter plays" or "serials" — short films released weekly that always ended at a crisis point. Japanese anime inherited this through manga's weekly chapter structure: *Weekly Shōnen Jump* chapters are approximately 19 pages and must end on a hook due to reader polls that determine which series continues. Low-polling series are cancelled — so cliffhangers evolved partly as a survival mechanism for manga authors. This economic pressure on narrative structure is uniquely Japanese and has no direct Western equivalent.

> 🔁 **If students are stuck:** "Think about it this way — you're at the end of an episode. The screen goes black. But you just shrug and go to sleep. What didn't the show do? What was missing?"

## 🌱 SLIDE 8 — TECHNIQUE SPOTLIGHT: CHARACTER DEVELOPMENT
### *My Hero Academia*
### ⏱ Estimated Time: 6 minutes

---

`[Advance to Slide 8. Play the MHA Character Development clip.]`

**"This video is a short academic essay disguised as a YouTube video — it was made by a creator called Storyteller's Notebook and it breaks down exactly how My Hero Academia constructs character development. Watch for: what specific moments in the story cause Deku to change?"**

`[Play clip. After:]`

**"Okay. What moments stood out to you?"**

`[Take 2–3 responses. Then guide:]`

**"Here is the rule the video is teaching, whether it says it directly or not: character development only counts if the change is earned. Earned means: the character had to struggle for it, make a sacrifice, or confront something they were afraid of."**

**"Deku's most important moment of development is not when he gets One For All — the power. It's when he has no power, stands in front of a Villain his body cannot fight, and runs toward the danger anyway. That moment of pure, powerless courage is what makes All Might choose him. The power follows the character, not the other way around."**

❓ **"What was the moment Deku truly changed as a person? And could that moment have happened in Episode 1?"**

`[Students often say "no." Push them to explain why:]`

❓ **"If it couldn't have happened in Episode 1 — what had to happen first? What did the audience need to know and feel before that moment could land?"**

`[This is the key question. The answer is: the audience needed to see Deku's helplessness, his bullying, his genuine love of heroes, his mother's sacrifice — all of it. Character development requires a setup longer than the change itself.]`

**"Now let's make this personal."**

❓ **"What is the internal flaw or fear that your anime character needs to overcome by the end of their arc? And what is the specific event — the trial — that forces them to confront it?"**

`[Give students 60 seconds to write this in their notebook. It feeds directly into the storyboard activity.]`

> 💡 **PhD Note:** Character development in anime is often analyzed through the lens of *mono no aware* (物の哀れ) — the Japanese aesthetic concept of the bittersweet awareness of impermanence, formalized by scholar Motoori Norinaga in the 18th century. In anime, characters don't simply "improve" — they are transformed by loss, and their development is measured by what they are willing to give up, not what they gain. Deku gains power but loses safety, innocence, and his body's health. This *cost of growth* is a distinctly Japanese narrative value that contrasts with Western hero narratives where the protagonist typically gains without losing.

## 📈 SLIDE 9 — TECHNIQUE SPOTLIGHT: NARRATIVE ARC
### *Naruto · One Piece · MHA*
### ⏱ Estimated Time: 6 minutes

---

`[Advance to Slide 9. Play the "What Is An Arc In Anime?" clip.]`

**"This is a conceptual video — it explains what an arc is and how anime uses them. As you watch, see if you can spot the five-stage structure we've been building toward."**

`[Play clip. After:]`

**"Let's apply the five-stage structure to Naruto's Chunin Exams arc — which runs from about Episode 20 to Episode 67."**

`[Walk through the timeline on the slide:]`

**"Introduction — teams enter the exams. Stakes are established: if you pass, you become a higher-ranked ninja. If you fail, you might not survive."**

**"Rising action — the Forest of Death. Three-person squads must survive 5 days in a forest full of A-rank ninja trying to steal their scrolls. Orochimaru appears and attacks Sasuke. The tension escalates continuously."**

**"Climax — Naruto versus Neji in the final tournament. This is the emotional peak of the entire arc. Neji believes fate is predetermined — that Naruto, a dead-last failure, cannot beat him. Naruto proves him wrong. His speech: 'I'll change your destiny' is one of the most famous lines in shonen anime. This is the moment the arc has been building to."**

**"Falling action — the village invasion begins. The arena erupts into chaos. The scope of the story expands. Orochimaru attacks the Third Hokage."**

**"Resolution — team bonds have been transformed. New arcs have been seeded. We end differently than we began."**

❓ **"Notice that the climax in the Chunin Exams is not a fight with the main villain — it's a fight about an idea. The fight is about whether fate is real. Why would the writer make the most important moment a battle about a concept rather than a physical battle?"**

`[Look for students who connect this to character development — the external fight mirrors the internal conflict.]`

**"That is the most important thing about narrative arcs: the external plot and the internal character arc should mirror each other. When Naruto defeats Neji physically, he is simultaneously defeating the idea of fate — the very idea that has been the internal obstacle of his entire life."**

❓ **"For your own character: what is the external conflict in their story? And what internal belief or fear does that external conflict force them to confront?"**

> 💡 **PhD Note:** The five-stage Freytag Pyramid structure (Introduction–Rising Action–Climax–Falling Action–Resolution) was formalized by German playwright Gustav Freytag in 1863 for classical five-act plays. It was absorbed into manga storytelling through Osamu Tezuka — Japan's "God of Manga" — who was deeply influenced by Disney animation and Western cinema in the 1940s–50s. One Piece author Eiichiro Oda has stated that he plots every arc's ending before writing the beginning — the same top-down structural discipline as a screenplay. Naruto author Masashi Kishimoto has said in NHK interviews that the Chunin Exams arc was specifically designed to prove that "the loser can win" — a direct inversion of the *noblesse oblige* value system of earlier shonen. This generational shift in shonen values is trackable entirely through arc structure.

## 🧩 SLIDE 10 — ALL FIVE TECHNIQUES IN ONE EPISODE
### *Demon Slayer Season 1 Episode 19*
### ⏱ Estimated Time: 5 minutes

---

`[Advance to Slide 10.]`

**"Everything we've discussed today — flashback, foreshadowing, cliffhanger, character development, and narrative arc — they don't exist separately. Great anime writers use all of them simultaneously, layered on top of each other."**

**"Demon Slayer Season 1 Episode 19 is the most famous example in modern anime history of all five techniques firing at once in a single 24-minute episode."**

`[Walk through each card:]`

**"Flashback — Tanjiro's memory of his father's dance arrives at the precise moment Tanjiro has no strength left. The past saves the present."**

**"Foreshadowing — The Hinokami Kagura dance was shown in Episode 1 as a New Year's ceremony. It seemed like cultural flavor. In Episode 19, it becomes the most powerful attack in the series. It was in front of us the entire time."**

**"Cliffhanger — The episode ends with Tanjiro completely out of power, Nezuko unconscious, and an undefeated demon standing over them. Audiences worldwide could not sleep. YouTube streams spiked. Twitter trended in Japan within the hour."**

**"Character development — Tanjiro, who at the start of the series could not even bring himself to kill the first demon he encountered because she reminded him of Nezuko, now makes the most violent and decisive choice of his life. We can trace every step of that change."**

**"Narrative arc — This episode is the climax of the entire Season 1 arc. Everything before it was rising action. The animation studio, ufotable, spent months on this single episode. It became one of the most-watched anime episodes in history."**

❓ **"Here is the big question: which of those five techniques hit you hardest as a viewer? And why do you think that specific technique was so effective for you personally?"**

`[This is the most important Socratic question of the lesson. There is no right answer. What you're listening for is the student's ability to connect a technique to their own emotional response — and articulate why.]`

`[Take 4–5 responses. Genuinely respond to each one. If a student says cliffhanger, ask: "What specifically were you afraid of losing?" If a student says flashback, ask: "Why did that particular memory matter to you?"]`

> 💡 **PhD Note:** Episode 19 of Demon Slayer is academically significant because it triggered what Japanese media scholars call a *media event* — a moment where a single piece of content forces collective cultural processing. The episode's Hinokami Kagura sequence was animated by Nozomu Abe and directed with techniques borrowed from Makoto Shinkai (*Your Name*) — specifically the "particle light" animation style that makes fire appear to breathe. The episode's virality (100M+ YouTube views within one month of its original 2019 airing) is documented in Japanese academic media studies as a case study in how streaming platforms have changed the relationship between animation quality and audience reach.

## ✏️ SLIDE 11 — STUDIO TIME: STORYBOARD ACTIVITY
### ⏱ Estimated Time: 15 minutes

---

`[Advance to Slide 11. Distribute art supplies: colored markers, drawing paper or sketchbooks.]`

**"This is your studio time. You are now going to do what manga writers call pre-production — designing a scene before you animate it."**

**"The task: Create a storyboard for an original anime scene. 4 to 6 panels. You must incorporate at least two of the five storytelling techniques we covered today. Label each technique in the margin of the panel where it appears."**

**"Use your course character if you already have one. If you don't have one yet — create someone right now. You have 15 minutes."**

`[Before they begin:]`

❓ **"Before your pencil touches the paper — what is the single emotion you want your viewer to feel at the end of your scene? Write that one word at the top of your page before you draw anything."**

`[This single instruction is the most important one — it gives every artistic decision a purpose. Walk around during work time and use the Socratic prompts on the slide:]`

`[When circulating, for students who look stuck:]`
**"Tell me out loud what happens in your scene. Don't draw yet — just tell me."**

`[For students who are rushing:]`
❓ **"You're moving fast. Which panel is the moment of highest tension? Is it in the right position — or does it need to move?"**

`[For students asking "how good does it have to look?"]`
**"Real anime storyboards from Studio Ghibli look like stick figures. Hayao Miyazaki's pre-production sketches are rough pencil drawings. Your job is clarity of story — not quality of art. Be bold."**

`[For students who finish early:]`
**"Go back to your first panel. If you removed it — what would the viewer not understand? If the answer is 'nothing,' then the first panel needs to change."**

> 💡 **PhD Note:** The Japanese term for storyboard is *e-konte* (絵コンテ) — literally "picture continuity." The technique was imported from American film production in the 1930s and became standard in anime production through Tezuka's studio, Mushi Production, in the 1960s. Hayao Miyazaki's e-konte for *Spirited Away* ran over 1,400 pages and was drawn before a single frame was animated. The storyboard is not a sketch — it is a complete pre-visualization of the emotional journey. Students who treat it as a rough "plan" are developing the same pre-production discipline used in professional studios.

## 🤝 SLIDE 12 — PEER SHARE: SOCRATIC GALLERY WALK
### ⏱ Estimated Time: 5 minutes

---

`[Advance to Slide 12.]`

**"We're going to do a gallery walk. Here is the protocol — it is three steps, and step one is silent."**

**"Step one: Look. Spend 60 seconds reading your partner's storyboard without saying a word. Look at the panels. Read the captions. Notice which techniques are labeled."**

`[After 60 seconds:]`

**"Step two: Name. Point to one specific panel and name the technique you see. Use the vocabulary. Start your sentence with: 'In panel __, I see __ because...'"**

**"Step three: Question. Ask one genuine question. Not 'I like this because...' — a real question. Use the starters on the board if you need them."**

`[After partner shares, facilitate a brief class discussion:]`

❓ **"Did anyone's partner point out something in your storyboard that you didn't realize you had done? What was it?"**

`[This almost always produces a beautiful moment — students discovering they had used a technique intuitively without naming it. When this happens, stop the class and say:]`

**"Let's pause on that. You used a technique without knowing you were using it. That means the technique is already in you — you've absorbed it from watching anime. What we're doing today is just giving you the name for what you already know how to feel."**

## 🎯 SLIDE 13 — EXIT TICKET
### ⏱ Estimated Time: 4 minutes

---

`[Advance to Slide 13.]`

**"Last four minutes. Notebooks out. Three questions — answer in full sentences."**

**"Question 1: Name one storytelling technique you found most effective today. Use the vocabulary term."**

**"Question 2: Explain why that technique enhances an anime's narrative. Be specific — name the anime and the scene."**

**"Question 3: Which technique will you use in your own anime character's story? How specifically will you use it?"**

`[Set a 3-minute timer. Circulate. Look for students who are writing vague answers — approach them and ask:]`
**"Which scene? Be specific. Name it."**

`[With 30 seconds left:]`

**"One more thing before you leave."**

**"Your out-of-class extension: Watch one episode of any anime this week — and log every storytelling technique you spot. Write the technique name, the timestamp, and why it worked or didn't work."**

**"Bring that log next class. It is your first piece of real anime analysis — and it is the foundation for your final project."**

`[Collect exit tickets OR have students show you their notebook before leaving.]`

## 📊 APPENDIX A — Assessment Guidance

| What You're Evaluating | What Strong Looks Like | What Needs Support |
|----------------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **Vocabulary use** | Student uses the term correctly in context and can define it without the slide | Student uses the word but in the wrong context |
| **Technique identification** | Student points to a specific moment in a clip and names the technique and its effect | Student names the technique but cannot locate it or explain its function |
| **Storyboard** | 2+ techniques labeled; captions explain emotional intent; character has a visible arc across panels | Single technique; captions are plot summary only; character is unchanged across panels |
| **Peer discussion** | Student builds on partner's idea, asks a real question, and uses vocabulary | Student only agrees or disagrees without reasoning |
| **Exit ticket** | Specific anime + scene named; causal explanation of technique effect; personal connection to own story | Vague reference to "an anime I like"; no causal explanation |

## 📊 APPENDIX B — Anticipated Student Responses & How to Handle Them

| Student Says | What It Tells You | How to Respond Socratically |
|-------------|------------------|----------------------------|
| "I don't watch anime" | Possible disengagement or unfamiliarity | "That's okay — let me describe the scene in words. What technique do you hear in that description?" |
| "Cliffhangers are just lazy writing" | Critical thinking — follow this thread | "What would a 'non-lazy' version of the same scene look like? How would you end the episode?" |
| "Foreshadowing only works if you rewatch" | Deep insight | "So is foreshadowing for the first viewing or the second? Can it be for both at the same time?" |
| "Deku is my favorite character" | Emotional connection | "What moment made you choose Deku? Was it before or after a major change in him?" |
| "I already used all five in my storyboard" | Possible overreach | "Show me the flashback panel. Tell me what information it reveals and exactly why the writer places it there." |
| "My character doesn't change" | Common mistake | "All great characters start broken. What is your character afraid of? That fear is where the development begins." |
