Anime Club — Final Project Presentation Day — Teacher Script (90 minutes)

Topic: Anime Appreciation & Analysis — "What Makes an Anime Truly Great?"

Below is a slide-by-slide spoken script. Stage directions are in italics. Time codes accumulate to 90 minutes.
Read it the way you'd actually say it to a room of kids who have worked hard all term —
proud, warm, energetic, and a little ceremonial. Today is their day. You're the hype person.
Slide 1 — Title & Driving Question (0:00 – 2:00)

[Open the deck. Stand center. Let the room settle. Don't rush this moment — it's their finale.]

"Welcome back, anime club. This is Lesson 10. The last one."

[Pause. Let that land.]

"Nine lessons. We've done conflict and character, history and globalization, Miyazaki and Megan Thee Stallion, outsider stories, soft power, and the $33 billion industry that started as ink on paper in postwar Japan. You've been taking notes, making connections, and — quietly — building toward today."

"Today you present."

[Tap the driving question on screen.]

"Here's the question that has lived underneath everything we've done: 'What makes an anime truly great — story, character, culture, or something deeper?' Keep that question in your head all day. You'll come back to it at the very end. Let's go."
Slide 2 — Do Now: Warm-Up Reflection (2:00 – 8:00)

[Click to the warm-up slide. Step to the side so they can see the screen clearly.]

"Before anyone presents, we open with a two-minute memory exercise. In your notebook — or on the sticky note I'm handing out — I want you to answer three questions silently:"

"First: think back across all nine lessons. What single anime moment stood out most to you? Not a whole series — one scene, one line, one image."

"Second: what made it memorable? Was it the story? A character decision? The animation? An emotion you didn't expect to feel from a cartoon?"

"Third: how does that moment connect to the anime you're about to present today? There should be a thread. Find it."

[Walk the room quietly while they write. Give a 30-second warning. Then:]

"Alright. Turn and talk — sixty seconds. Share your moment with the person next to you."

[After sixty seconds, cold-call two students. Don't evaluate the content yet — just validate and reflect it back.]

"Notice what just happened. You just did the exact thing every great presenter does before they go on stage — you reminded yourself why this matters."
Slide 3 — Vocabulary Review: The Four Analytical Terms (8:00 – 14:00)

"Before we hear any presentations, let's sharpen the lenses. Four terms on screen. You've seen all of these before. Today they're not vocabulary — they're your scoring criteria."

[Read each term aloud. Point at the screen. Don't just recite — explain why each one matters today.]

    Presentation — sharing your work and ideas with an audience in a structured, clear way. Today, structure means your audience can follow your argument without getting lost. Clear means they don't have to guess what you think.

    Analysis — breaking down a work to understand how its parts create meaning and feeling. Not 'this anime is good.' Instead: 'this specific scene works because the color palette shifts from warm to cold exactly when the protagonist stops trusting people.' That's analysis.

    Critique — thoughtful judgment of both strengths and areas to improve. This one you'll use during the feedback session. It takes courage to give critique and skill to receive it. We'll practice both.

    Evaluation — judging quality using clear, fair criteria and evidence. When you fill out the feedback form, you're not saying 'I liked it.' You're evaluating against criteria. There's a difference.

"Quick check. On your sticky note, write down which of these four you feel most confident doing today — and which one feels shakiest. One word each. That's your personal growth edge for this class."

[Give them ninety seconds. Don't collect it — it's for them. Keep moving.]

"Alright. Now the framework that holds everything together."
Slide 4 — The Four Pillars of Analysis (14:00 – 20:00)

[Click to the pillars slide. Read each header, then expand. Keep energy up — this is a review, but it's also setting the bar for what you expect to see in every presentation.]

"Four pillars. These are the four lenses through which we've analyzed every anime this term, and they're the four dimensions every presenter needs to cover today. Let me remind you what each one actually means at depth:"

    Storytelling — plot structure, conflict, resolution, pacing. The question isn't 'what happens?' It's 'how does the story put pressure on the characters? What does the pacing tell us about what the creator values? Is the conflict external, internal, or both?'

    Character Development — growth arcs, motivation, relationships. The question isn't 'who is the main character?' It's 'who were they at the beginning, what changed them, and who are they now?' If you can't answer all three, you haven't gotten to character development yet.

    Animation Style — color, linework, movement, visual metaphor. This is the one most presenters skip. Don't. The animation is not decoration — it's argument. The color palette is an opinion. The frame composition is a choice. Tell us what that choice means.

    Thematic Depth — cultural roots, social ideas, universal themes. This is where we zoom out. What is this anime actually about beneath the surface? What does it say about being human? What part of it is specifically Japanese, and what part lands everywhere?

[Point to the two reference examples on the slide.]

"Two examples on screen — Spirited Away and My Hero Academia. Look at how the Spirited Away example uses all four pillars in a single paragraph. That's the model. Your presentation should do the same thing for your anime."

[Give a sixty-second silent read of the examples, then:]

"Any questions before I show you the feedback rubric? Last chance to ask without a peer audience."

[Take one or two quick questions. Then move.]
Slide 5 — Presentation Setup: Materials & Hook (20:00 – 28:00)

"Alright — setup time. Here's what we're doing in the next eight minutes."

[Project the three setup steps.]

"Step one: grab your materials. Poster board, slides, art supplies, printed visuals, a clip cued up — whatever you prepared. Get it out now. If you're going digital, make sure your tab is open and ready. We are not troubleshooting tech at showtime."

[Give them two minutes to get settled. Circulate. Check that anyone doing a video clip has it loaded.]

"Step two: know your four pillars. You have to hit all four — storytelling, character development, animation style, thematic depth. If your current outline skips one, you have about four minutes right now to patch it. Look at your notes."

[Give them ninety seconds of silent review time.]

"Step three — and this is the one that separates a good presentation from a great one — practice your hook. Your first sixty seconds. You have three things to land before anything else: what anime you chose, why you chose it over everything else you've watched, and one key claim — one argument about why this anime matters."

[Give them two minutes to draft or rehearse the hook quietly. Some will mouth it to themselves. That's perfect.]

"On the sticky note in front of you, draft your opening hook right now. One sentence: 'I chose [anime], because [reason], and I'm going to argue that [claim].' Write it. Own it. That's how you open."

[Walk the room. Peek at hooks. If someone's claim is too vague — 'because it's a great story' — push them gently: 'Great how? Give me the argument.']
Slide 6 — Feedback Rubric Walkthrough (28:00 – 33:00)

[Project the rubric table. Hand out printed feedback forms if you haven't already.]

"Before the first person presents, everyone needs to understand the feedback criteria. This is not a favor sheet. It's not applause. It's analytical feedback — and it's more valuable to the presenter than your clapping."

[Walk through each row of the rubric out loud. Point at the screen.]

    Storytelling — three-star response: clearly explains plot structure and conflict. Two-star: describes the plot but needs more depth. You're listening for: did they explain HOW the conflict creates tension, or did they just tell us what happened?

    Character — three-star: shows character arc with specific evidence. Two-star: names characters but doesn't show growth. Key word: specific. 'Naruto grows up' is two stars. 'In episode 133, when Naruto refuses to kill Sasuke, we see how his bond has become stronger than his desire for recognition' — that's three stars.

    Culture — three-star: connects to Japanese culture AND global impact. Two-star: mentions culture but stays surface-level. You want to hear: a specific Japanese cultural element, and then a reason it lands universally.

    Creativity — three-star: uses visuals or clips effectively, engaging delivery. Two-star: presentation is clear but less dynamic. This doesn't mean you need to be a performer. It means: did you make choices that helped us understand, or did you just stand there and read?

"One more thing: fill out the feedback form for every presenter. Not just your friends. Everyone deserves feedback, and everyone learns from giving it."

[Hold up the form and point to the three fields: Presenter's name. One strength. One question it raised.]

"One strength. Not a list of strengths — one. The most specific, truthful one you can name. And one question — something the presentation genuinely made you wonder. That's it."
Slide 7 — 🌟 Student Presentations (33:00 – 65:00)

[This block is the heart of the class. Structure it firmly so it doesn't run loose.]

[Before calling the first presenter:]

"Here's how this works. Each presenter gets five to seven minutes — including setup. When I hold up one finger, you have sixty seconds. When I hold up a closed fist, wrap the sentence you're on and close. Everyone else: fill out your feedback form during the presentation. No side conversations. You are the audience this person deserved."

[Call presenters by a pre-set order — alphabetical, random draw, volunteer first. Your choice. Have the order ready.]

[During each presentation, do three things:]

    [Listen for the four pillars. If a presenter clearly misses one — say, animation style — make a mental note to address it gently in the feedback round, not by interrupting.]

    [Watch the room. If students disengage, lean forward yourself. Your body language sets the standard for respect in this room.]

    [Write one genuine, specific strength per presenter. You'll need it.]

[After each presentation, lead a thirty-second applause and then immediately hand the floor to peer feedback — not your own commentary yet. Your turn comes at the end.]
Slide 8 — Peer Feedback Session (65:00 – 76:00)

[After all presentations are complete, click to the peer feedback slide.]

"Alright. Every presenter just put their work in front of this room. Now it's time to honor that with real feedback. Not 'it was good.' Real."

[Walk through the four feedback sentence frames on screen. Say them out loud with emphasis.]

    Start with a strength: "I really appreciated how you explained…" — and then finish it with something specific. Not 'how you explained your anime.' 'How you explained why the color red only appears in the final arc of the show.' That's a strength.

    Ask a question: "I'm curious — what made you choose this anime over others?" Or: "You mentioned the cultural element once — could you say more about why it matters globally?" A good question is a gift.

    Offer a suggestion gently: "One thing that might strengthen it — could you add a clip that shows the animation shift?" Not 'you should have done this.' Could you add. That word matters.

    Be specific: Not 'it was good' — 'the clip you chose for the transformation scene was powerful because it let us see the character's expression without any dialogue. That's a strong analytical choice.'

[Give four minutes of silent written feedback — one form per presenter they watched closely.]

[Then open to a structured verbal share-out:]

"I want three people to give verbal feedback to one presenter — one strength, one question. Watch how it feels to receive specific, honest feedback instead of vague praise. Presenters: listen. You don't have to respond right now. Just receive it."

[Facilitate two or three exchanges. Keep it moving. Intervene if feedback is harsh or vague.]
Slide 9 — Three Big Questions: The Room Debates (76:00 – 83:00)

[Click to the three discussion questions. Keep energy high — you're almost at the finish line.]

"Last discussion of the term. Three questions. I want three different voices — three people who haven't spoken yet today. Ready?"

[Cold-call for each question. Don't let the same two students dominate.]

    Question one: "If anime stopped being made in Japan — but kept being made globally — would it still be anime?" Listen for: does the term belong to the origin, or to the aesthetic? Push back gently on whatever they say. 'What if I said the opposite — what breaks in your argument?'

    Question two: "As global pop culture adopts anime aesthetics, does Japan gain or lose cultural power?" Listen for: soft power gains from influence, but authenticity can erode when something is universalized. Both are true. Push them to hold that tension.

    Question three: "What global topic would YOU put at the center of your own anime story? Why that theme?" This one is personal. Accept any answer. Your job is to ask one follow-up: 'Why does that topic need anime to tell it — why not a film, a book, a song?'

[After three responses, step back and land it:]

"What you just did — argue with evidence, hold competing ideas at once, ask why a medium matters for a message — that's not a school skill. That's how critics, creators, and scholars think. You've been doing it all term. You didn't notice."
Slide 10 — Exit Ticket & Final Wrap (83:00 – 90:00)

[Click to the exit ticket slide. Lower your energy slightly — this is reflective, not hyped.]

"Last thing. Exit ticket. Two prompts. Take three minutes:"

    "Write down one new insight you gained from a peer's presentation today — something you didn't know or hadn't thought about before."

    "How did that insight change or expand your understanding of anime? Not 'I learned that' — 'now I think differently about [X] because…'"

[Give them three minutes of silence. Circulate. If a student writes one-word answers, crouch down and ask them to expand one sentence — quietly, not a call-out.]

[When time is up, stand center again.]

"Before you drop that in the box — I want to say something. You started this club learning what anime is. You're leaving it knowing what anime does: it builds characters who mirror our outsider selves, it carries cultural values across oceans, it rewired film and music and fashion and tech, and — as every single presentation today proved — it creates genuine analysis when you treat it seriously."

"Anime is not just entertainment. It is a window into humanity. What you learned to see through that window this term — story, character, culture, and something deeper — you'll keep seeing for the rest of your life."

"Drop your ticket. You're dismissed. Great work."

[Stand at the door. Shake hands or fist-bump each student as they leave. It matters.]
Take-Home Challenge (Distribute at Exit)

"Pick a new anime series or film you've never watched. Apply the four-pillar framework — storytelling, character development, animation style, thematic depth. Come back ready to argue whether it belongs in the canon of truly great anime, and why."
Teaching Tips Throughout

    Pacing rule: the presentation block (Slide 7) is sacred time. Guard it from over-talking. Your setup work in Slides 3–6 is what makes the presentations go well. Don't rush setup.

    Cold-call always: every slide before the presentations should produce at least one student voice. The warm-up, vocabulary check, hook draft, and rubric walkthrough should all have student contributions.

    Energy management: you will peak in the warm-up, drop to focused during setup, rise for presentations, go deep during feedback, and rise again for the debate and wrap. Consciously modulate — don't run at 100% all 90 minutes or the room will burn out.

    The feedback form is non-negotiable: if a student skips it, they skip their own learning. Make it clear in your setup that forms are collected at the end.

    Protect every presenter: if the room gets restless during a quieter presentation, lean in, make eye contact with the presenter, nod — your attention is the room's permission to pay attention.

    If you run long: compress the verbal feedback round (Slide 8) to written only, and skip the live debate on one of the three big questions. The exit ticket on Slide 10 is non-negotiable.

    If a presenter skips a pillar: don't interrupt. Prompt during Q&A: "Can you say more about the animation choices — what did the visual style do for the story?"

    The final send-off line matters: end every class with — "Anime is not just entertainment. It is a window into humanity." Students will remember the ritual.
