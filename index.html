Lesson 2

Exploring Anime Genres & Themes

Why do we reach for the same type of story again and again?

Today we explore anime genres and themes — the invisible architecture behind every story we love.

Discussion goal

Use evidence from clips, sketches, and class discussion to explain how genre shapes what an audience expects and what a story can say.

Creative goal

Pick a genre for your own anime character and begin connecting your story seed to a real theme that reflects something cultural or personal.

Do Now · Reflect & Share

What is your favorite anime — and why?

Jot down 2–3 reasons in your notebook. Think beyond plot — consider how it made you feel and what kind of story it is.

?Writing prompts

  • What kind of main character does it have?
  • Does it make you laugh, cry, feel inspired, or think hard?
  • Is there a theme or message underneath the story?
  • What genre do you think it belongs to — and why?

🤝Partner share

  • Share your favorite and one reason with a partner.
  • Notice if you both picked the same genre without knowing the word for it yet.
  • If you have never seen anime, describe a story you love in any format — film, book, or TV show.

Teacher move: collect genre guesses on the board before defining anything — revisit them at the end of class.

Learning Targets

What we are building today

By the end of this lesson

  • Define and identify at least six anime genres and their key characteristics.
  • Analyze common themes in anime and explain how they connect to Japanese culture.
  • Critically discuss how genre guides audience expectations.

Connecting to the course

  • Genre is a container for literary devices — knowing it helps you write with intention.
  • Themes are the why behind the story; you'll need one for your own character design.
  • Analyzing genre and theme builds the media-reading skills used in every future lesson.

Vocabulary · Use These Words Today

The language of genre

Genre

A category of story defined by shared elements, tone, and audience — the map readers use before they open the book.

Theme

A central idea or message a story explores, such as identity, belonging, or perseverance.

Shonen

Action-focused stories aimed at young men — often centered on growth, friendship, and overcoming challenges. (Naruto, My Hero Academia)

Shojo

Stories emphasizing emotion, relationships, and self-discovery — traditionally aimed at young women. (Fruits Basket, Sailor Moon)

Isekai

A protagonist is transported to another world, often fantastical — a genre about re-invention and escape. (No Game No Life, That Time I Got Reincarnated)

Slice of Life & Mecha

Slice of life — everyday moments, emotional realism. (K-On!, Haikyuu!!)
Mecha — giant robots, technology, humanity vs. machine. (Gundam, Mecha-Ude)

Teacher move: after each definition, ask "Can you name a non-anime story — a book, film, or game — that fits this genre?"

Deep Dive · Themes & Cultural Roots

Where do anime themes come from?

Common themes & their cultural roots

Perseverance (ganbaru)

A deeply held Japanese value — to do your best regardless of outcome, reflected in sports and shonen anime.

Group over self

Team dynamics, clubs, and squad bonds drive many stories — loyalty to a group is a central Japanese social value.

Respect for nature & spirits

Shinto and Buddhist traditions give rise to anime full of nature spirits, creatures, and moral balance. (Studio Ghibli)

Identity & belonging

From outcasts finding their place (Naruto) to transformation (Isekai) — the struggle to belong recurs across every genre.

?Discussion prompts

  • Pick one theme from the list. Can you find it in an anime you watch or know? Where exactly does it appear?
  • Could the same theme appear in a story from your own culture? How might it look different?
  • Does knowing the cultural root of a theme change how you feel about a scene you have already seen?

Teacher move: invite students to add a theme the list missed based on their own anime knowledge.

Studio Time · Genre Sketch

Pick a genre — then draw the scene it promises

Drawing directions

  • Choose one genre from today: Shonen, Shojo, Isekai, Slice of Life, or Mecha.
  • Sketch a simple scene or character that embodies that genre's visual style and energy.
  • Add one visible detail that hints at the theme you want to explore.
  • Label the genre and write one sentence describing the theme at the bottom.

Guiding questions while circulating

  • "If someone who had never seen anime looked at your sketch, what genre would they guess?"
  • "Where in the drawing is the theme showing — and how?"
  • "Does your character belong to their world, or are they trying to break out of it?"

Goal link: this sketch becomes the genre and theme foundation for the original anime character students will develop throughout the course.

Writing · Short Reflection

Connect your genre to Japanese culture

Reflection prompt (5–7 minutes)

Write 4–5 sentences in your notebook:

  1. Name the genre you chose and one of its defining characteristics.
  2. Identify the theme in your sketch and explain what it means.
  3. Describe one way this theme connects to something in Japanese culture or values.
  4. Explain how your character embodies — or pushes against — that theme.

Use at least two vocabulary words from today.

Peer share (3 minutes)

  • Read your reflection to a partner.
  • Partner responds: "One thing that worked was… One question I have is…"
  • Swap and repeat.

Closing question for the room: "Did anyone change their mind about a genre after hearing a partner's reflection?"

Exit Ticket · Reflection

Genres, themes, and you

Before you leave, respond in complete sentences using at least two vocabulary words and one specific example from a clip we watched:

1. One new genre or theme I learned about today is … and it changes my view of anime because …

2. The genre I chose for my own story is … because it lets me explore the theme of …

3. One question I still have about how genre or theme connects to Japanese culture is …

Out-of-class extension: explore a new genre on Crunchyroll or MyAnimeList and write a short review focusing on its themes and cultural elements. Share on the class forum.
