"You have all seen artwork that just feels right — a movie poster,

💡 [DEEP CONTEXT — deliver slowly, with weight]:
"Here is the truth that anchors everything today. A finished image
is really two separate things wearing one costume. The first is the
CONTENT — the trees, the person, the sky. The second is the
STRUCTURE — how that content is organized, both in the file and on
the canvas. Layers are the structure inside the file. Composition is
the structure on the canvas. Amateurs think only about content.
Professionals think about all three. Today you become professionals."

SLIDE 5 — KEY VOCABULARY
[Duration: ~3 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the six vocabulary cards: Layers, Composition, Foreground,
Background, Balance, Contrast. Don't read definitions mechanically —
tie each to something the warm-up already showed.]

"Six words. You will use every one of them today, with your hands,
not just your ears."

"LAYERS — transparent sheets stacked on top of each other, each
holding separate elements you can edit independently. COMPOSITION —
the arrangement of those elements within the frame. FOREGROUND — the
part closest to you, usually largest and at the bottom. BACKGROUND —
the furthest part: sky, horizon, distant scenery, usually smallest
and softest."

"BALANCE — how visual weight is distributed across the image.
CONTRAST — the difference between light and dark, or between colours,
that makes things stand out."

💡 "Pay attention to a hidden pattern here. Three of these words —
layers, foreground, background — are about SPACE and STRUCTURE. The
other three — composition, balance, contrast — are about ATTENTION:
where the viewer's eye goes and how it feels getting there. Great art
controls both. You manage space with layers; you direct attention
with composition. Same artwork, two completely different jobs."

✋ [Quick check]: "Without looking — what is the difference between
   a LAYER and the FOREGROUND?" [Listen for: a layer is a structural
   sheet in the file; foreground is a spatial zone in the image. A
   foreground usually lives ON its own layer, but they are not the
   same idea. Clarify if the distinction is fuzzy.]

SLIDE 6 — WHAT ARE LAYERS? (THE SANDWICH)
[Duration: ~4 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the layer-stack visual — Background, Middle Ground,
Foreground — and the sandwich/burger image. Point to each layer in
the stack as you speak.]

"Think of layers like a sandwich. Each ingredient sits on its own
level. You can pull out the tomato without touching the cheese. That
is the whole magic of digital layers: every layer holds separate
elements you can move, edit, or delete WITHOUT affecting the others."

"Look at the stack on screen. Layer 1 at the bottom: the BACKGROUND —
sky, horizon, distant elements. Layer 2 in the middle: the MIDDLE
GROUND — hills, trees, buildings in the distance. Layer 3 on top: the
FOREGROUND — the closest objects, largest and most detailed. Stack
them, and you have a scene with real depth."

💡 [DEEP EXPLANATION]:
"Here is the genuinely important idea, and it has a name:
NON-DESTRUCTIVE EDITING. On a single flat layer, painting a tree over
the sky destroys the sky underneath forever — the pixels are gone.
But on separate layers, the tree floats above the untouched sky. Hide
the tree, and the sky is perfectly intact. Nothing was destroyed.

This is why professionals never work on one flat layer. A movie poster
might have two hundred layers. A character illustration separates the
line art, the flat colors, the shading, and the highlights onto
different layers — so the artist can recolor the shirt at 11 PM
without repainting the entire character. Layers turn permanent
decisions into reversible experiments. And experiments are where good
art comes from."

[Point to the eye icons in the layer-stack visual:]

"See the little eye icon on each layer? That toggles visibility on and
off without deleting anything. It is the single most-used button in
all of digital art. Remember it — you'll use it in ten minutes."

✋ [Ask students]: "If you merged all your layers into one — what is
   the one thing you would permanently lose the ability to do?"
   [Expected: the ability to move or edit any single element
   independently. Merging is a one-way door — emphasize this.]

💡 [SYNTHESIS]:
"Look at the benefits listed: edit one part without affecting others,

SEPARATION. Layers separate decisions from each other. When your
decisions are separated, every one of them stays changeable. That
freedom to keep changing things is what lets art evolve from a rough
sketch into something polished. A flat image is a finished sentence.
A layered file is a conversation you can keep having."

SLIDE 8 — FOREGROUND, MIDDLE GROUND, BACKGROUND
[Duration: ~3 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the three zone cards and the landscape photo. Trace the
three depth bands on the image with your finger.]

"Three zones create the illusion of depth on a flat screen.
FOREGROUND: closest to the viewer — largest elements, most detail,
highest contrast, sits at the bottom. MIDDLE GROUND: in between —
medium size, moderate detail, the bridge that connects near to far.
BACKGROUND: furthest away — small, least detail, softer colours: sky,
distant hills, horizon."

💡 [DEEP INSIGHT — atmospheric perspective]:
"Why does the background get softer and lighter? This is not just an
art rule — it is physics. The air between you and a distant mountain
is full of tiny particles that scatter light. The further away
something is, the more air you're looking through, so distant objects
lose contrast, lose detail, and shift toward pale blue. Leonardo da
Vinci named this 'atmospheric perspective' over five hundred years
ago, and it is why distant hills always look hazy blue-gray.

So when you make your background softer and your foreground sharper,
you are not decorating — you are recreating how human vision actually
works. The viewer's brain reads 'soft and pale equals far away'
automatically. You are speaking directly to their visual instincts."

✋ [Point to the photo]: "Can you spot all three zones in this image?
   Where exactly does the foreground end and the middle ground begin?"
   [Let them debate the boundary — there's often no hard line, and
   that ambiguity is itself a useful lesson about real scenes.]

💡 "Here is the rule to carry into your own work: DEPTH IS A

sharp, dark tree in front of a small, soft, pale mountain — THAT has
depth, because the two are in conversation. Depth lives in the
contrast between layers, never in any one layer by itself."

SLIDE 10 — COMPOSITION INTRO
[Duration: ~3 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the composition intro with the Balance / Contrast / Focal
Point cards and the rule-of-thirds image. This is the pivot from
STRUCTURE to ATTENTION.]

"We've handled the file structure — layers and depth. Now we turn to
the canvas itself. COMPOSITION is the art of arranging elements so
the viewer knows exactly where to look — and feels something when
they look there."

"Three tools do most of the work. BALANCE: visual weight feels evenly
distributed, so nothing seems to be falling off one side. CONTRAST:
differences in light, size, and colour create focus. FOCAL POINT:
every strong composition has ONE main element the eye finds first."

💡 [DEEP EXPLANATION — composition as a path]:
"Here is the mental model that changes everything. A composition is
not a static arrangement — it is a PATH you design for the viewer's
eye. The eye enters the image, lands on the focal point first, then
travels through the supporting elements in an order YOU control. Bad
composition leaves the eye wandering, lost, unsure where to settle.
Good composition is a guided tour where you are the tour guide.

Every tool we cover — balance, contrast, the Rule of Thirds — is
really an instrument for controlling that journey. You are not
decorating a rectangle. You are directing where a human being looks,
and for how long. That is real power, and it is learnable."

✋ [Quick poll]: "In the image on screen — where did YOUR eye go
   first? Point at it." [Most will point at the same spot. Use that:
   "That agreement is not coincidence — the artist engineered it.")]

SLIDE 11 — BALANCE
[Duration: ~3 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the two balance diagrams — symmetrical and asymmetrical.
Point to each visual block layout as you describe it.]

"Two kinds of balance. SYMMETRICAL: both sides mirror each other.
This creates calm, formality, and order — think portraits, logos,
mandalas, grand buildings. ASYMMETRICAL: different elements balance
each other through size, colour, and position. This creates dynamic
tension and energy — and it feels more natural and alive."

💡 [DEEP INSIGHT — visual weight]:
"The key concept hiding inside balance is VISUAL WEIGHT. Elements have
weight in your eye even though they weigh nothing on a screen. A large
object is heavier than a small one. A dark object is heavier than a
light one. A bright, saturated red is heavier than a dull gray. An
element of high detail is heavier than a plain one.

Asymmetrical balance is the art of trading these weights. One big pale
shape on the left can be balanced by one small intense-red dot on the
right — different elements, equal pull. It's a seesaw, but instead of
balancing pounds, you're balancing attention. This is why
asymmetrical balance feels alive: the eye senses the tension being
held, like a gymnast frozen mid-motion."

[Point to the Pro Tip card:]

"And here's why this matters for YOUR work: asymmetrical balance is
used in roughly 85% of professional digital illustrations. Perfect
symmetry can feel static and stiff. A little imbalance, carefully
held, is what makes an image feel like it's breathing."

✋ [Ask students]: "Symmetrical or asymmetrical — which feels more
   exciting to you, and which feels more peaceful? There's no wrong
   answer — the point is they DO different emotional jobs."

SLIDE 12 — CONTRAST
[Duration: ~3 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the contrast cards and the HIGH/low contrast comparison
boxes. Have students physically squint at the two boxes.]

"Contrast is the DIFFERENCE between two elements. High contrast is
easy to see and grabs attention. Low contrast is subtle and calm.
Three kinds matter most: VALUE contrast — light versus dark, the most
powerful of all. COLOUR contrast — opposite colours like blue and
orange, which vibrate against each other. And SIZE contrast — a tiny
object beside a huge one instantly tells the viewer what's important."

[Point to the HIGH-contrast vs low-contrast boxes:]

"Look at these two boxes. White text on black — instantly readable.
Pale gray text on slightly-paler gray — your eye strains. Which is
easier to read? Now SQUINT at both. The high-contrast one survives
the squint. The low-contrast one disappears."

💡 [DEEP INSIGHT — the squint test]:
"That squint you just did is a professional technique. When you squint,
you blur away color and detail and see only VALUE — the pattern of
light and dark. Squinting at your own artwork reveals whether your
focal point actually stands out, or whether everything is mushing
into the same gray middle.

Here is the master rule that ties contrast to everything else today:
the human eye is hard-wired to look at the point of HIGHEST contrast
first. So contrast is not just decoration — it is your steering wheel.
Put your highest contrast exactly where you want the eye to land, and
reduce contrast in the background to push it quietly back. High
contrast at the focal point, low contrast in the distance. Master
that one sentence and you control the entire viewer's journey."

✋ [Point to the Remember card]: "So the strategy is: maximum contrast
   at your focal point, reduced contrast in your background layers.
   Say it back to me — where does your highest contrast go?"

SLIDE 13 — RULE OF THIRDS
[Duration: ~4 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Display the Rule of Thirds grid overlaid on the portrait. Point to
each of the four intersection "power points" with your finger.]

"Here is the single most useful composition tool you'll learn today.
Divide your canvas into nine equal parts — two lines across, two lines
down, like a tic-tac-toe board. Now look at the four points where
those lines CROSS. Those are your POWER POINTS. Place your most
important element on or near one of them."

"The DOs and AVOIDs are on screen. DO place your subject along a line
or at an intersection. AVOID dead-center placement — it feels static
and lifeless. TIP: align your horizon line with the top third OR the
bottom third line, never straight across the middle."

💡 [DEEP EXPLANATION — why thirds, and a caution]:
"Why does off-center feel more alive than centered? A dead-center
subject is perfectly resolved — there's nowhere for the eye to travel,
no tension, nothing unresolved. An off-center subject creates a little
gap of open space, and that gap creates implied movement and breathing
room. The eye has somewhere to go. Tension, not resolution, is what
holds attention.

Now, intellectual honesty: the Rule of Thirds is a simplification of a
deeper idea. Some artists use the Golden Ratio — about 1 to 1.618 —
which places focal points slightly differently. And the Rule of Thirds
is a guideline, not a law. Symmetry can be breathtaking when used on
purpose — think of a perfectly centered cathedral reflected in still
water. The rule is your training wheels: follow it until you
understand WHY it works, and then you've earned the right to break it
deliberately."

✋ [Ask students]: "Look at the portrait with the grid. Is the
   subject's eye sitting near a power point? Most great portraits put
   the EYES on the upper third line. Why the eyes specifically?"
   [Expected: eyes are where human attention naturally goes — so we
   place them where the composition naturally pulls the gaze.]

💡 [CONCEPTUAL DEPTH — stacking order]:
"The most important idea in any layers panel is STACKING ORDER. The
layer at the TOP of the panel sits in FRONT in the image. The layer
at the BOTTOM of the panel is the furthest BACK. So your panel is
literally a side-view of your scene's depth: background at the bottom
of the list, foreground at the top.

This means reordering layers — just dragging them up or down — instantly
changes what's in front of what. A bird painted on a layer below the
mountain is hidden; drag that layer above, and the bird flies in front.
You are not redrawing anything. You are reshuffling depth. That is the
superpower flat images can never have."

add three layers, name them, draw a simple shape on each, then
reorder them and watch the front-to-back relationship flip."

================================================================================
APPENDIX A — COMMON PITFALLS & HOW TO HANDLE THEM
================================================================================

PITFALL: Student merges all layers early and then can't move anything.
  Handle: "That's the lesson, painfully. Merging is a one-way door —
          it flattens your separated decisions back into one
          un-editable sheet. Undo it now if you can (Ctrl+Z). Going
          forward, only merge at the very end, when you're 100% sure.
          Until then, keep everything separate. Separation is freedom."

PITFALL: Student's focal point keeps drifting to dead center.
  Handle: "Turn on the grid overlay — Canva and Photopea both have
          one. Now physically drag your subject onto a power point and
          feel the difference. Center is the default the eye finds
          boring; off-center is a choice the eye finds interesting.
          Make the choice on purpose."

PITFALL: Student says 'I can't draw, so my art is bad.'
  Handle: "Look at the assessment criteria — not one of them is
          'drawing skill.' Your shapes can be simple rectangles and
          circles. What we grade is layer use, focal clarity, thirds,
          balance, contrast — all DECISIONS, not drawing. A circle in
          the right place with the right contrast beats a beautiful
          drawing dead-center. Composition is the great equalizer."

PITFALL: Student's background overpowers the foreground.
  Handle: "Squint at it. See how the busy background fights your
          subject? Drop the background layer's opacity, or lower its
          contrast and detail. Remember atmospheric perspective —
          distant things should be soft and pale. Push it back so the
          foreground can win."

PITFALL: Student confuses 'layer' with 'foreground.'
  Handle: "A layer is the STRUCTURE — a sheet in your file. The
          foreground is a ZONE — the near part of the scene. You put
          your foreground ON a foreground layer, but you could also
          put it on three layers, or put two zones on one layer. The
          layer is the container; the zone is the content. Keep them
          separate in your head."

PITFALL: Student can't find the Layers panel in Canva.
  Handle: "Canva hides it. Click any element first, then click
          'Position' in the top toolbar, then the 'Layers' tab. It's
          buried because Canva is built for non-artists — but the
          concept is identical to Photopea. Find the doorway once and
          you'll never lose it."

APPENDIX B — EXTENSION QUESTIONS FOR ADVANCED STUDENTS
================================================================================

1. ATMOSPHERIC PERSPECTIVE: Da Vinci observed that distant objects lose
   contrast and shift blue. Recreate this digitally using only opacity
   and a blue overlay layer. At what opacity does a hill start reading
   as 'far away'? Is the effect linear or does it accelerate?

2. VISUAL WEIGHT AS PHYSICS: If a large pale shape balances a small
   intense-red shape, can you express balance as a formula — weight =
   size × intensity? Test it: make a composition that's mathematically
   balanced but FEELS off. What variable did the formula miss?

3. THE GOLDEN RATIO vs THIRDS: The Rule of Thirds puts power points at
   1/3 and 2/3. The Golden Ratio puts them at roughly 0.382 and 0.618.
   Overlay both grids on the same image. How far apart are the power
   points? Can the human eye even tell the difference?

4. GAZE PREDICTION: Composition controls where the eye goes first.
   Show three classmates your piece for two seconds each, then ask what
   they saw first. Did your intended focal point win? If not, where did
   your contrast actually peak?

5. ETHICS OF ATTENTION: Composition is the science of directing human
   attention. Advertising and social media use these exact tools to
   capture your eye. Where is the line between guiding a viewer and
   manipulating one? Should artists think about that responsibility?

APPENDIX C — QUICK REFERENCE: KEY PRINCIPLES
================================================================================

Layer Stacking Order:
  TOP of panel    = FRONT of image (foreground)
  BOTTOM of panel = BACK of image (background)
  Build back-to-front; name every layer.

Three Depth Zones:
  Foreground  → largest, sharpest, highest contrast, bottom of canvas
  Middle ground → medium size, medium detail, the connector
  Background  → smallest, softest, palest (atmospheric perspective)

Rule of Thirds:
  Divide canvas into 3 × 3 grid.
  4 power points = the 4 line intersections.
  Place focal point ON a power point; horizon ON a third-line.

Balance:
  Symmetrical   → calm, formal, ordered (mirror images)
  Asymmetrical  → dynamic, alive (visual weights traded; ~85% of pro work)
  Visual weight = size × value × saturation × detail

Contrast (your steering wheel):
  Value (light/dark) > Colour (opposites) > Size.
  HIGH contrast at the focal point. LOW contrast in the background.
  The eye looks at highest contrast first. Use the squint test.

The One-Sentence Master Rule:
  Separate your elements onto layers, place your focal point on a
  power point, and put your highest contrast there — and the viewer's
  eye will go exactly where you want it.
