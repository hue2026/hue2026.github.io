📖 Key Vocabulary

Words to Know

Layers 🗂️

Transparent sheets stacked on top of each other. Each holds separate elements you can edit independently.

Composition 🖼️

The arrangement of visual elements within a frame to create a pleasing or purposeful image.

Foreground 🌿

The part of the image that appears closest to the viewer — usually at the bottom and largest in size.

Background 🌄

The furthest part from the viewer — sky, horizon, distant scenery. Usually smallest and least detailed.

Balance ⚖️

The visual weight distribution in an image. Can be symmetrical (mirror) or asymmetrical (different but equal).

Contrast 🔲

The difference between light and dark, or between colours — helps focal elements stand out clearly.

📚 What Are Layers?

Think of layers like a sandwich 🥪

Each ingredient sits on its own "level." You can remove the tomato without affecting the cheese — just like digital layers! Every layer holds separate elements you can move, edit, or delete without touching the others.

👁️

Layer 1 — Background

Sky, horizon, distant elements

👁️

Layer 2 — Middle Ground

Hills, trees, buildings in distance

👁️

Layer 3 — Foreground

Closest objects — largest & most detailed

🌄 Foreground · Middle Ground · Background

Three Zones Create Depth

🌿

Foreground — Closest to viewer

Largest elements, most detail, highest contrast. Sits at the bottom of the canvas.

🏘️

Middle Ground — In between

Medium-sized elements with moderate detail. Connects foreground to background.

🌄

Background — Furthest away

Small elements, least detail, softer colours. Sky, distant hills, horizon.

🖼️ Composition

What makes an image feel right?

Composition is the art of arranging elements in your canvas so the viewer knows exactly where to look — and feels something when they do.

⚖️ Balance — Visual weight

Elements feel equally distributed so nothing feels like it's "falling off" one side

🔲 Contrast — Difference

Light vs dark, large vs small, detailed vs plain — contrast creates focus points

🎯 Focal Point — The star

Every strong composition has ONE main element the eye is drawn to first

Source: invaluable.com — Rule of Thirds in Art

⚖️ Balance in Composition

Two types of balance

Symmetrical Balance

Both sides are mirror images of each other. Creates a sense of calm, formality, and order. Think: portraits, logos, mandalas.

Can you think of a famous symmetrical artwork? 🤔

Asymmetrical Balance

Different elements balance each other through size, colour, and position. Creates dynamic tension and energy. More natural and interesting.

Which type feels more exciting to you? 🤔
💡 Pro tip: Asymmetrical balance is used in 85% of professional digital illustrations — it feels more alive!
🔲 Contrast

Contrast makes things pop!

Contrast is the difference between two elements. High contrast = easy to see. Low contrast = subtle and calm. Use it deliberately to guide the viewer's eye.

🌓 Value Contrast

Light vs dark — the most powerful contrast. White text on black = maximum readability

🎨 Colour Contrast

Opposite colours (red/green, blue/orange) create vibrant, attention-grabbing contrast

📐 Size Contrast

A tiny object next to a large one tells the viewer immediately what's more important

🔲 Rule of Thirds

Divide your canvas into 9 equal parts

Place your most important element at one of the 4 intersection points (power points). This creates natural, dynamic composition that feels engaging — not boring and centered.

✅ Do: Place your subject along the lines or at intersections

❌ Avoid: Dead-center placement — it feels static and uninteresting

💡 Tip: Align your horizon line with the top or bottom third line

Source: invaluable.com — Rule of Thirds in Art

  • Experiment with 5+ composition techniques
👀 Good vs Poor Composition

Can you spot the difference?

✅ Strong Composition
  • Main subject at Rule of Thirds intersection
  • Clear foreground, mid, background layers
  • Asymmetrical balance — feels dynamic
  • High contrast on focal element (dark house)
❌ Weak Composition
  • Dead-center placement — feels static
  • No foreground/background — flat depth
  • No size variation — everything equal
  • No contrast to create a focal point
