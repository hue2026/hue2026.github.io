Anime Club Lesson 6 — Teacher Script (60 minutes)

Topic: The History of Anime & Its Evolution (1907 – Today)

Below is a slide-by-slide spoken script. Stage directions are in italics. Time codes accumulate to 60 minutes. Read it conversationally — the script is written the way you'd actually say it to a room of curious kids.
Slide 1 — Title (0:00 – 0:30)

[Open the deck. Stand near the screen. Smile.]

"Welcome back, anime club! Today is Lesson 6, and it might be the most important one yet — because today we travel back in time. We're going to find out where anime actually came from. Not Naruto, not Demon Slayer — I mean the very, very beginning. Two seconds of black-and-white animation found in a flea market. By the end of today, you'll know how a tiny experimental film from 1907 grew into a billion-dollar global art form. Ready? Let's go."
Slide 2 — Do Now: Watch & Notice (0:30 – 5:00)

[Click play on the embedded 3:40 video, "History of Anime — Part 1."]

"Here's your Do Now. We're watching a short 3-minute-40-second video. Your job: notice three things that surprise you. Don't write yet — just watch. We'll talk after."

[Let the video play. Stand to the side, watch with them. When it ends:]

"Okay — turn and talk to your neighbor for thirty seconds. What surprised you? What did you not expect?"

[Listen in. Pull two students to share aloud. Validate:]

"Great noticing. Hold those ideas — we're going to confirm and expand all of them in the next 50 minutes."
Slide 3 — Key Terms (5:00 – 8:00)

"Before we time-travel, four quick vocabulary words. These are the tools we'll use all class."

    Anime — Japanese animated film or TV. In Japan it just means "animation," but globally it means anime style.

    Manga — Japanese comics. Almost every anime starts as manga first.

    Cel animation — old-school: drawings on transparent plastic sheets, photographed one frame at a time. Twelve drawings for one second of motion.

    Mangaka — a manga artist. Like saying "novelist," but for manga.

"Repeat after me — anime, manga, cel, mangaka. Good. These words are your passport for today."
Slide 4 — 1907–1940s: Where It All Began (8:00 – 13:00)

[Point at the timeline and the vintage manga image on the right.]

"1907. Picture this: in a Kyoto flea market in 2005, somebody finds a tiny film reel. They play it. It's two seconds long — a little boy drawing kanji on a chalkboard. That's Katsudō Shashin, and it's the oldest known Japanese animation. Older than Mickey Mouse by twenty-one years."

"Ten years later, 1917, three artists — Kitayama, Kouchi, and Shimokawa — start making short films inspired by Western animators. Japan is officially in the animation game."

"Then jump to the 1940s. World War II. The Japanese government funds an animation called Momotarō's Divine Sea Warriors — Japan's first feature-length anime. But here's the catch: it's propaganda. Made to convince Japanese children the war was good."

[Pause. Tap the context-question card.]

"So my question to you — type or jot it down — why might a government want to use animation during wartime? Think about audience, emotion, and trust. I'll give you sixty seconds."

[Cold-call two students. The right answer territory: kids trust cartoons, animation hides reality behind cute art, emotional manipulation is easier in drawings than live action.]
Slide 5 — Osamu Tezuka & Astro Boy (13:00 – 17:00)

[Switch to slide 5.]

"Okay — meet the most important person in anime history. Osamu Tezuka. He's called the God of Manga. In 1963, he creates a TV show called Tetsuwan Atom — you know it as Astro Boy — and that's the moment anime, as we know it, is born."

"Three things Tezuka invented that we still use today:"

    Big expressive eyes. He borrowed them from Disney's Bambi. That's why anime characters have huge eyes — it makes emotion readable.

    Limited animation. He couldn't afford 24 drawings per second like Disney, so he used 8. Reuse cycles. Hold frames longer. He turned a budget cut into a style.

    Serial storytelling. Each week, the story continues. That's why anime has long arcs — Tezuka invented that habit.

"Astro Boy was also the first anime exported to America, in 1963. So when your grandparents say 'I watched cartoons as a kid,' they might literally mean anime."
Slide 6 — 1970s–1980s: Japan Goes Global (17:00 – 22:00)

"After Tezuka, the floodgates open. The 70s and 80s are anime's adolescence — louder, weirder, more ambitious."

    Mobile Suit Gundam (1979) — giant robots, but with politics. War is messy. Heroes have doubts. This is anime growing up.

    Akira (1988) — a feature film that costs 11 million dollars. Hand-drawn. 327 different colors when most films used 50. It blows minds in Tokyo and New York. Akira is the moment Hollywood realizes — wait, anime can do things we can't.

    Sailor Moon and Dragon Ball — late 80s into the 90s — these are the shows that hook the global audience. Suddenly there are American kids drawing transformation sequences in their notebooks.

"Big idea here: anime stops being 'Japanese cartoons' and starts being a global language."
Slide 7 — Studio Ghibli & Spirited Away (22:00 – 27:00)

[Show slide 7 with the Spirited Away poster.]

"1985. Two animators, Hayao Miyazaki and Isao Takahata, start a studio called Ghibli. Their philosophy is the opposite of fast TV anime — they hand-draw everything, slowly, beautifully, with nature and silence and emotion."

"In 2001, Ghibli releases Spirited Away. A ten-year-old girl, parents turned into pigs, a bathhouse for spirits. In 2003, it wins the Academy Award for Best Animated Feature. That's the first non-English film to ever win that Oscar."

"Why does Ghibli matter? Because they prove anime is art — not just entertainment. Forests have spirits. Wind matters. Silence is allowed. And kids around the world cry watching it."

[Optional: ask, "Has anyone here seen a Ghibli film? Which one? What did you feel?"]
Slide 8 — 1990s–2000s: Cel → Digital (27:00 – 32:00)

"Now the technology revolution. For 80 years, every anime frame is hand-drawn on a clear plastic cel and photographed. Slow. Expensive. Unforgiving — one mistake means redrawing the whole cel."

"Then in the late 90s, computers get fast enough. Toy Story lands in 1995 and the entire animation world realizes — we don't need plastic anymore. By the early 2000s, almost every anime studio in Japan switches to digital."

"What changes?"

    Color counts go from hundreds to millions.

    Studios produce more episodes per year.

    Digital effects — glowing energy, lens flares, 3D backgrounds — become normal.

"But something is also lost. Hand-drawn cels had little imperfections, paint texture, warmth. Some fans miss that. Which is exactly the cel-vs-digital debate you'll do later in this lesson."
Slide 9 — 2010s–Now: Anime Goes Truly Global (32:00 – 36:00)

"Last era — the one you're living in. Three big shifts:"

    Streaming. Crunchyroll, Netflix, Hulu. You can watch a Tokyo show the same day it airs in Japan. That used to take years.

    Global hits. Attack on Titan, Demon Slayer, Jujutsu Kaisen, My Hero Academia. These trend on TikTok worldwide.

    Demon Slayer's Mugen Train (2020) — the highest-grossing Japanese film ever, and one of the highest-grossing animated films in history. Anime is no longer niche. It's mainstream culture.

"Industry value today: more than $30 billion. Bigger than the entire NFL."
Slide 10 — Naruto (2002) Case Study (36:00 – 41:00)

[Click the Naruto thumbnail to play the 1:30 opening video.]

"Listen to this opening. Watch the visuals."

[Let it play — about 90 seconds.]

"Naruto, 2002. A loud orange ninja kid that nobody believes in. Why is it the perfect example of modern anime?"

    Conflict & growth — every arc, Naruto fails, then trains, then succeeds. That's the shounen formula.

    Teamwork over individual genius — remember collectivist culture from the slides? Naruto wins because of friends. Always.

    Long-form serialized storytelling — 720 episodes. That's only possible because of the digital era.

"Naruto is what happens when you take Tezuka's recipe and run it through 40 years of evolution."
Slide 11 — My Hero Academia (2016) (41:00 – 43:00)

"Quick comparison. My Hero Academia, 2016. Same shounen DNA — kid with a dream, found family, growth — but now mixed with Western superheroes. American comic-book influence flowing back into Japanese animation. Anime is now in conversation with the whole world. The arrows go both ways."
Slide 12 — Build Your Anime Timeline (43:00 – 45:00)

[Activity slide.]

"Two-minute speed activity. On the timeline on screen, drag or write one anime per era. 1960s — what? 1980s — what? 2000s — what? 2020s — what? Go."

[Walk the room. Spot-check answers. Sample correct: 60s = Astro Boy, 80s = Akira, 2000s = Naruto, 2020s = Demon Slayer.]
Slide 13 — Why Does Japan Tell Stories This Way? (45:00 – 48:00)

"Now the deepest question. Why does Japanese animation feel different from American? Four reasons."

    Post-WWII trauma. Tezuka survived the firebombing of Osaka. That's why so much anime is anti-war or about peaceful technology.

    Shinto. The native religion teaches every river, tree, and stone has a spirit. That's why Ghibli forests are alive.

    Economic boom and crash. The 1980s bubble funded Akira. The 1990s crash birthed Evangelion — a show literally about teenagers crushed by depression.

    Collectivist culture. Heroes win through teamwork. Naruto needs Sakura and Sasuke. Deku needs Class 1-A. That's not accidental — it's cultural.

"Look at the Akira poster on screen. Eleven million dollars. 327 colors. That film is bubble-economy Japan in one image."
Slide 14 — Mini Poster: Your Anime Era (48:00 – 50:00)

"Two-minute mini poster sketch. Pick any anime. On a sticky note or in your notebook write: title, era, one historical context fact, and one cultural connection. We'll share next class — but I want a draft on paper before you leave."
Slide 15 — Three Big Questions (50:00 – 53:00)

"Pull-it-together moment. Three questions. I want one student to answer each."

    What did Tezuka invent that we still use today? (Big eyes, limited animation, serial stories.)

    What changed when anime went from cel to digital? (More colors, faster production, less hand-drawn warmth.)

    Why did anime go global? (Streaming, cultural curiosity, universal themes of growth and friendship.)

[Cold-call. Affirm. Correct gently.]
Slide 16 — Before You Go… Exit Ticket (53:00 – 56:00)

"Exit ticket. One sentence each:"

    One thing you learned today.

    One thing you're still curious about.

    The anime you most want to watch because of this lesson.

"Drop it in the box on your way out. I read every single one."
Slides 17–22 — Optional Extension Activities (use as time allows)

If you finish early or want bonus engagement, choose one of these to extend:

    Slide 17 — Era Detective: Show a clip with no title. Students guess the decade based on art and tech.

    Slide 18 — Cel vs. Digital T-Chart: Pros and cons. Great for debate.

    Slide 19 — Create-Your-Era pitch: Imagine the 2030s — what does anime look like?

    Slide 20 — Global Impact Web: Where do you see anime outside anime? (Fashion, games, language, film.)

    Slide 21 — Peer interview: Pair up. Ask each other what era they'd live in.

    Slide 22 — Argument Builder: "The most important era of anime is _ because _." Three pieces of evidence.

Slide 23 — Sketch Your Anime Character (56:00 – 59:00)

"Final three minutes — and this is the fun part. Use the canvas. Sketch a character that belongs to a specific era. 1960s = simple lines, big eyes. 1980s = mecha. 2020s = wild colors, dynamic poses. Don't worry about being good. Worry about being era-accurate."
Slide 24 — Wrap (59:00 – 60:00)

"Here's the takeaway, club: anime is 117 years old. It started as a 2-second flicker of a boy at a chalkboard. It survived a world war, a recession, a digital revolution, and a global pandemic. Every show you love is standing on the shoulders of Tezuka, Miyazaki, and a thousand artists you've never heard of."

"Next class we're going deeper into one era. Vote on the way out — Ghibli, mecha, or modern shounen. Thanks, club. Dismissed."
Teaching Tips Throughout

    Pacing rule: never spend more than 5 minutes on any single slide except videos.

    Cold-call always: every slide should produce at least one student voice.

    Use the images: slides 4, 7, 10, 13, 20 have visuals — point at them, don't just stand near them.

    Save the Akira moment for slide 6 OR 13 emphasis — it's your most "wow" data point ($11M, 327 colors).

    If you run long: cut slides 17–22, keep 23–24. The exit ticket is non-negotiable.
