Anime Club Lesson 7 — Teacher Script (60 minutes)

Topic: Anime Goes Global — Cultural Exchange, Soft Power, and the World's New Pop Language

Below is a slide-by-slide spoken script. Stage directions are in italics. Time codes accumulate to 60 minutes. Read it the way you'd actually say it to a room of curious kids — warm, fast, a little dramatic where it counts.
Slide 1 — Title & Driving Question (0:00 – 1:00)

[Open the deck. Stand at the front. Hold for a beat before speaking.]

"Welcome back, anime club. Lesson 7. Last time we did the history — 1907, Tezuka, Ghibli, Naruto, all of it. Today we ask the bigger, weirder question: 'If anime was made in Japan, why does the whole world feel like it belongs to them?' That's our driving question. Hold onto it. Every slide today is a clue."

"By the end of class, you'll be able to argue — with evidence — whether anime is still Japanese, or whether it has quietly become the planet's shared visual language."
Slide 2 — Do Now: Notice & Wonder (1:00 – 5:00)

[Click play on the embedded opening video. Step to the side.]

"Do Now. Watch this clip. Two columns in your notebook — Notice on the left, Wonder on the right. I want at least three of each. No talking, just looking."

[Let it play. When it ends, give 30 seconds of silent writing time, then:]

"Turn and talk — thirty seconds. Share one Notice and one Wonder with your neighbor."

[Cold-call two pairs. Validate. Don't correct yet — just collect.]
Slide 3 — Six Key Terms (5:00 – 9:00)

"Six vocabulary words today. These are the lenses we'll use all class — without them, today's conversation falls apart."

    Globalization — cultures, economies, and media spreading worldwide

    Cultural Exchange — ideas, art, or customs crossing between communities

    Pop Culture — entertainment everyone consumes, from kids to grandmas

    Cross-media Influence — when one art form, like anime, shapes another, like film or fashion

    Iconography — the recognizable symbols of a culture or genre, the spiky hair, the speed lines

    Soft Power — a country's influence through culture, not force or money

"Don't copy the slide — re-write each one as if you're explaining it to a friend who has never watched anime. That's the assignment. Last question on this slide: which term best explains why anime went global? Pick one. We'll vote in two minutes."

[Quick show of hands. Most kids will say globalization or soft power — both correct, push them to defend it.]
Slide 4 — By the Numbers: The $33B Industry (9:00 – 13:00)

[Point at the stats panel.]

"Numbers matter, so listen carefully. Anime is a $33 billion industry, up from $25B in 2020. 150 million Netflix households watch anime. 80% of viewing happens outside Japan. It's available in 190 countries on Netflix alone."

"Fastest-growing markets? Southeast Asia plus 42%, Latin America plus 38%, Middle East plus 29% — all between 2020 and 2023. The most-watched globally are Attack on Titan, Demon Slayer, One Piece, Naruto, and My Hero Academia."

"Now your job: fill in the four-row table. For each stat — what does it mean in plain English? Why is it surprising? What question does it raise? Three minutes."

[Walk the room. Spot-check. Close with: "Which region surprised you most, and why?" Cold-call two students.]
Slide 5 — Anime in Western Film (13:00 – 19:00)

"Now we get into the proof. Four Western films — all enormous — that cannot exist without anime."

    Avatar: The Last Airbender — creators openly cited Dragon Ball Z, FLCL, and Miyazaki. It changed what an American cartoon was allowed to be.

    Spider-Man: Into the Spider-Verse — used manga Ben-Day dots, speed lines, and 12-fps "anime smears." Won the 2019 Oscar for Best Animated Film.

    Creed III, 2023 — Michael B. Jordan cited Hajime no Ippo and Baki. First mainstream Hollywood film to openly use anime fight grammar.

    The Matrix, 1999 — the Wachowskis storyboarded scenes from Ghost in the Shell and screened it for Warner Bros. executives to pitch the movie. Bullet-time? Borrowed from anime.

[Tap the "Think About It" card.]

"My question: without anime, would these films look the way they do? Take 60 seconds. Write yes or no, then one sentence of why."

[Cold-call. The point you're driving at: anime isn't influencing Western film at the edges. It's in the bones.]
Slide 6 — Anime vs. Western Animation T-Chart (19:00 – 23:00)

"Compare-and-contrast time. Five rows: art style, storytelling, characters, cultural roots, famous example. Anime column, Western column, and the third column is your opinion — which do you prefer and why? "

[Give them four minutes. Then:]

"Last question on this slide: what do both have in common? Don't skip it — that's the most important box. The answer territory: both want to make you feel something. Both use heroes. Both use color emotionally. The medium is the same — the accent is different."

[Bonus prompt:] "Spider-Verse borrowed from anime. Was that homage, influence, or appropriation? Vote with your feet — left wall, middle, right wall."
Slide 7 — Spirited Away & Soft Power (23:00 – 28:00)

[Show the Spirited Away panel.]

"Roger Ebert in 2003: 'Miyazaki films exist outside the usual anime box. They're cinema.' Spirited Away won the Oscar in 2003 — first non-English animated film to ever do that."

"Universal themes, check all you see — coming of age, greed, environment, family, identity. Then name one specifically Japanese element in the film — bathhouses, kami, the train across the sea — and ask yourself: would removing it weaken the film? "

[Color-analysis micro-activity.] "Pick a scene. Warm tones or cool tones? What emotion is the color carrying? Thirty seconds."

[Big question to land the slide:] "Soft power question. When a Japanese film wins a Hollywood Oscar, who benefits more — Japan or Hollywood? Defend your answer in one sentence."
Slide 8 — Hip-Hop × Anime (28:00 – 33:00)

"Now the fun one. Music."

    Kanye West × Akira, 2010 — the Stronger music video used Akira footage directly. The whole Graduation visual language is cyberpunk-Akira.

    Megan Thee Stallion — calls herself a "Houston hottie Naruto runner." Her My Hero Academia and Demon Slayer collabs sold 2 million units in 48 hours.

    Michael Jackson — hired Akira's director Katsuhiro Otomo for the HIStory tour, and was reportedly developing an anime film when he died.

    Cowboy Bebop's soundtrack — Yoko Kanno's jazz-fusion has 200M+ Spotify streams, and anime OSTs now chart on Billboard.

[Tap the Think About This prompt.] "Both hip-hop and anime were once seen as niche or underground. What do they have in common? Talk for sixty seconds with a partner."

[Listen. The answer territory: outsider art, emotional honesty, both built by people the mainstream ignored, both scaled through the internet.]

"Fill in the three-row music table — artist, anime reference, type of connection, and the hardest column: cultural exchange or appropriation? "

[Closing vote:] "'Anime is just entertainment — not real cultural exchange.' Agree, disagree, or it depends? "
Slide 9 — Fashion, Streetwear & Cosplay (33:00 – 37:00)

"Fashion is where soft power gets worn."

    Streetwear collabs — Nike × Dragon Ball Z, Uniqlo × Evangelion, Supreme × Akira, Louis Vuitton × Murakami. Anime merch is now a $15B+ industry.

    Cosplay economy — $6.7B globally, 40 million active cosplayers, and 60% of convention costumes are anime.

    Harajuku → global — Lolita, Fairy Kei, Visual Kei all started in Tokyo and now show up from São Paulo to Seoul.

[Personal hook.] "Have you ever worn anime-inspired clothing? What did it mean to you — fandom, identity, just looked cool? Two volunteers, share."

[Two-minute design challenge.] "On a sticky note: pick an item — shirt, bag, sneaker. Pick an anime. Name your target audience. Go."

[Closing vote:] "Wearing anime merch is — fandom, fashion, cultural appreciation, or appropriation? "
Slide 10 — The Outsider Story: Naruto Case Study (37:00 – 42:00)

[Click the Naruto opening — about 90 seconds.]

"Watch. Listen. Don't write yet."

[After it plays:]

"Three quick reflection questions on the slide:"

    "What emotion does the opening create in the first 10 seconds? "

    "What visual or sound choice hits hardest, and why? "

    "What does the animation style tell you about Naruto before you know the story? "

"Now the big idea. Naruto is the kid nobody believed in. That story resonated across Latin America, Africa, and Southeast Asia because every culture has a version of the outsider. Lil Uzi Vert, 21 Savage, and Offset have all cited Naruto. The Naruto run became global — 720,000 people RSVP'd to Storm Area 51 doing it."

[Connect to their lives.] "Naruto's exclusion is about something he was born with. What does that represent in real life? Disability, class, race, religion, neurodivergence — there's no wrong answer if you can defend it."
Slide 11 — My Hero Academia & Quirk Inequality (42:00 – 45:00)

"Quick parallel. Deku is born without a power in a world where 80% of people have abilities. Western fans read that as a mirror for disability, class inequality, and privilege."

[Partner activity.] "Pair up. Which outsider story hits harder — Naruto or Deku, and why? Then ask your partner: what real-world issue does your favorite anime secretly explore? Two minutes."

[Reflection prompt:] "On the way back to your seat — how did your partner's perspective challenge yours? One sentence."

[Class Question Generator:] "Each pair, write one question for the whole class based on your conversation. We'll pull two at random."
Slide 12 — Anime in Games & Tech (45:00 – 49:00)

"Anime didn't just shape film and music. It rewired technology."

    JRPGs — Final Fantasy, Dragon Quest, Persona, all anime-coded. $8B genre.

    Pokémon — $150B franchise. Most valuable media IP on Earth. 440M+ games sold.

    Mecha → real robots — Gundam shaped Boston Dynamics design, and Japanese engineers cite Evangelion as bipedal-locomotion inspiration.

    VTubers — Hololive's anime-avatar streamers have 70M combined subscribers.

    AI Art — most image-generation models are trained on anime datasets, and "anime-style" is the #1 prompt on Midjourney and Stable Diffusion.

    Genshin Impact — a Chinese anime-style game made $4.4B in year one, proving anime aesthetics now belong to the world, not only Japan.

[Push-further table.] "Fill in the four-row table — Pokémon, mecha, AI art, Ghibli-Minecraft. Plain English. So what? What does it prove about anime's power? "

[Sentence stem to land the slide.] "When someone says 'anime is just cartoons,' you'd now say… finish the sentence."
Slide 13 — Debate: Is Anime Still Japanese? (49:00 – 53:00)

"This is the big debate. The driving question, finally answered — by you."

"Two stances on screen: YES, it stays Japanese, or NO, it's becoming global. Pick a side. Then build a four-part argument: claim, evidence (1–2 facts from today), reasoning, counter-argument."

[Four minutes silent writing. Then sample two from each side. Don't declare a winner — protect both stances.]

"Notice what just happened. Both sides used facts from today's slides. That means the question isn't which side is right — it's which framing tells you more about the world."
Slide 14 — Where Do You See Anime? (53:00 – 55:00)

"Look around your life. Six categories on screen — Film & TV, Fashion, Music, Games, Language, Merch."

"Think beyond the obvious — sports, memes, school slang, your friendships, your career dreams. In two minutes, jot down at least one example per category. We'll share three out loud."
Slide 15 — Mini Poster Pitch (55:00 – 57:00)

"Mini poster. Draw a scene, symbol, or character that represents how anime changed the world. Add a one-liner hook that would make someone stop and look at your poster."

"Be ready to pitch in 60 seconds: what anime, which industry, one key stat, your argument. We'll do two pitches now and the rest next class."

[Pick two volunteers. Time them strictly. Snap to celebrate.]
Slide 16 — Three Big Questions (57:00 – 58:30)

"Pull-it-together moment. Three questions, three different students."

    "If anime stopped being made in Japan tomorrow but kept being made globally — would it still be anime? "

    "As global pop culture adopts anime aesthetics, does Japan gain or lose cultural power? "

    "Based on today, what global topic would YOU put at the center of your own anime story? "

[Cold-call. Affirm. No need to resolve — let the questions linger.]
Slide 17 — The K-On! Soft Power Case (58:30 – 59:30)

"One last micro-story before you go. K-On! is an anime about a high school light-music club. Sounds small. Watch the chain reaction:"

"Music → language learning → tourism → cultural ambassador. There are active K-On! tribute bands in Brazil, Indonesia, Germany, and the USA. Members learned Japanese to understand the lyrics. Tourism to filming locations grew 400% after the film. That is exactly what soft power looks like in real time."

[One reflection prompt.] "Has an anime ever made you want to learn something new — an instrument, a language, a skill, a recipe? Hold that — it's your exit ticket."
Slide 18 — Exit Ticket & Wrap (59:30 – 60:00)

"Exit ticket — four lines, one sentence each:"

    "📚 I learned… "

    "🔗 This connects to my anime… "

    "❓ I'm still wondering… "

    "✏️ For my own anime story… "

"Drop it in the box on your way out. Homework — pick one industry anime influenced, find three more facts we didn't cover, and bring your most surprising discovery next class."

"Takeaway, club: anime started as a Japanese art form. Today it's the closest thing the planet has to a shared visual language — sneakers, hip-hop, robots, video games, AI art, the kid in São Paulo learning Japanese from a guitar anime. That's not entertainment. That's soft power. And you're fluent in it. Dismissed."
Teaching Tips Throughout

    Pacing rule: never spend more than 5 minutes on any single slide except videos and the debate.

    Cold-call always: every slide should produce at least one student voice.

    Use the panels: slides 4, 5, 8, 9, and 12 are stat-heavy — point at the numbers, don't just read them.

    Save the wow-stat for emphasis: "$33B, 80% outside Japan, 190 countries" — drop one of those whenever energy dips.

    Protect both sides of the debate on Slide 13 — your job is rigor, not a verdict.

    If you run long: cut Slide 17 (K-On!) and the second mini-poster pitch. The exit ticket on Slide 18 is non-negotiable.
