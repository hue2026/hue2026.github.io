SLIDE 1 — TITLE SLIDE & LEARNING OBJECTIVES
[Duration: ~3 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[As students settle in, display the slide. Begin with energy and a genuine smile.]

"Good morning, everyone — welcome back to Anime Club! Today's lesson
is one that I have been genuinely looking forward to, because we are
going to tackle the most fundamental question in all of storytelling."

"Here it is: Why do some characters feel real? Why do certain characters
live in your head for years after you finish a series — while others
you forget the moment the credits roll?"

"The answer is CHARACTER DESIGN — not the visual design, though that
matters too — but the deeper design: who a character IS, how they
THINK, what they WANT, and most importantly, how they CHANGE."

💡 [DEEP CONTEXT — deliver this slowly and with conviction]:
"Character is the engine of every great story. Aristotle — a philosopher
who lived over two thousand years ago — wrote that in great stories,
everything exists to serve character. Plot is just a series of tests
that reveal who a character really is. Theme is what a character's
choices say about the human condition. Even visual design — the colors
of a character's clothes, their hairstyle, the shape of their eyes —
is crafted to communicate personality before a single word is spoken.

When we talk about a character being 'well-written,' we are making
a claim about craft — the specific techniques and choices a writer
made to create the illusion of a real, complex human being. Today
you are going to learn those techniques. You'll never watch anime
the same way again."

[Point to the 'You will be able to...' section of the slide]

"By the end of today, you'll be able to do two things:
  1. Describe ANY character using precise vocabulary
  2. Explain WHY they matter to the story — not just that you like them

Those two skills are what separates a fan from a literary critic.
You're going to be both today. Let's go."


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
SLIDE 2 — WARM-UP: WHO IS YOUR FAVORITE CHARACTER?
[Duration: ~8 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[Read the activity prompt with warmth — this should feel inviting, not like a test]

"Let's start with YOU. This is personal. There are no wrong answers.
Take out your notebook — we're doing a three-step activity."

"STEP 1: Pick a character.
Write the name of your absolute favorite anime character. It can be from
anything — Naruto, My Hero Academia, Demon Slayer, Haikyuu!!, Spirited
Away, One Piece, Attack on Titan, Studio Ghibli, anything at all. The
only rule: it has to be YOUR favorite, not the one you think sounds
coolest. Your real answer."

[Give students 60 seconds to write]

"STEP 2: Describe them in 3 words.
Not what they look like — what they ARE LIKE. Their personality. Three
adjectives. Go."

[Give students 90 seconds. Circulate and peek — this tells you a lot about their prior knowledge]

"STEP 3: The big question.
Write one more sentence: did this character stay the same throughout the
whole series? Or did they CHANGE? And if they changed — how?"

[Give students 90 seconds to write. Some students may need prompting here]

[PROMPTING HINTS if students are stuck]:
"Think about who they were in Episode 1 versus who they were at the end.
Did they get stronger — not just physically, but emotionally? Did they
change their mind about something they believed deeply? Did they lose
something that changed them forever?"

💡 [DEEP CONCEPT — introduce this AFTER they write, not before]:
"Here's why I asked that third question specifically. In literary theory —
the academic study of stories — characters are divided into two fundamental
types:

  STATIC characters: They do not change. Their personality, values, and
  worldview at the end of the story are the same as at the beginning.
  This is NOT always bad — some of the most beloved characters in fiction
  are static. Sherlock Holmes barely changes across 60 stories. He is a
  fixed point that the story REVOLVES around.

  DYNAMIC characters: They undergo a genuine internal transformation.
  Something in the story forces them to grow, break, rebuild, or completely
  reverse who they were. This transformation is called a CHARACTER ARC.

Most great anime protagonists have dramatic character arcs — and
understanding that arc is the key to understanding what the story is
actually ABOUT."

[Display the share prompt]

"Now — turn to the person next to you. 2 minutes. Share your character
and your three words. Then ask your partner: did THEIR character change?
In what way?"

✋ [Give pairs exactly 2 minutes. Then call on 3-4 students to share ONE
   observation with the class — not their whole analysis, just one
   surprising or interesting thing they noticed in the discussion.]

[Listen actively and validate responses — use student answers as bridges
to the next section]

"What I'm hearing is fascinating. Some of you picked characters who
grew stronger and braver. Some picked characters who started confident
and got humbled. Some picked characters who barely changed at all but
somehow carried the emotional weight of an entire series. Every one of
those is a legitimate character design strategy — and today we're going
to understand WHY each one works."


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
SLIDE 3 — KEY VOCABULARY: 5 WORDS THAT UNLOCK CHARACTER
[Duration: ~5 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

"Before we go deeper, we need five vocabulary words. Not ten. Not twenty.
Five. These are the professional tools that writers, critics, and
screenwriters actually use. Learn these five words and you can discuss
any character in any story in the world."

"WORD 1: TRAIT — A consistent feature of a character's personality.

  💡 A trait is something that shows up repeatedly. Naruto is not just
  SOMETIMES determined — he is ALWAYS determined, even when he is losing
  badly, even when everyone tells him to give up. That persistence is a
  trait. Traits are the building blocks of character. A fully realized
  character typically has three to five core traits that influence every
  decision they make. Real people are the same — psychologists call this
  PERSONALITY CONSISTENCY, the tendency for our core tendencies to shape
  our behavior across very different situations."

"WORD 2: MOTIVATION — What a character WANTS, and why they want it.

  💡 This is where beginning writers most often go wrong. They describe
  what characters DO without explaining why. But motivation is the source
  of all meaningful action. Screenwriting professors teach a famous rule:
  'Motivation explains behavior, and behavior reveals character.'
  In anime, motivations are often stated explicitly — 'I want to be
  Hokage' — but the deeper, more interesting motivation is almost always
  unstated. Naruto doesn't just want political power. He wants to be
  SEEN. He wants to belong. He wants proof that he exists and matters.
  That emotional truth beneath the surface goal is called the
  WOUND or INTERNAL MOTIVATION, and it's what makes audiences cry."

"WORD 3: ARC — The journey of change a character goes through.

  💡 An arc is not just a change — it is a MEANINGFUL change that emerges
  from everything the character experiences. The story's events are like
  a forge: they apply pressure and heat to a character, and what comes
  out on the other side is either refined or broken. In narrative theory,
  a complete character arc typically has three movements:
    - The WOUND: what happened before the story that shaped the character
    - The DESIRE: what they are chasing throughout the story
    - The TRANSFORMATION: how the story changes who they are at their core
  Tanjiro Kamado, for example, begins with a wound (his family's murder),
  a desire (to cure his sister), and transforms into someone who finds
  compassion even for the demons who hurt him. That arc IS Demon Slayer."

"WORD 4: CONFLICT — The force that challenges a character and DRIVES the arc.

  💡 No conflict, no story. This is not an exaggeration — it is a
  structural truth. Conflict is the engine of narrative. In literary
  analysis, conflict takes five classic forms:
    - Character vs. Character (Izuku vs. Bakugo)
    - Character vs. Self (Eren Yeager's entire arc)
    - Character vs. Society (basically all of My Hero Academia)
    - Character vs. Nature (characters surviving the physical world)
    - Character vs. Fate (characters defying destiny — classic anime theme)
  The deepest, most resonant conflicts are internal — character vs. self —
  because they mirror the universal human experience of wanting to be
  better than we are."

"WORD 5: THEME — The BIG IDEA the character's story is trying to tell us.

  💡 Theme is the answer to the question: 'So what?' A story can be
  about a boy who wants to be a ninja — that's plot. But THEME asks:
  what does this story say about FRIENDSHIP? About LONELINESS? About
  what it costs to be extraordinary in a world that fears you?
  Theme is always about the human condition — the universal experiences
  of love, loss, belonging, power, and identity. The reason anime
  resonates globally across language and culture barriers is that the
  best anime tells universal human truths through fantastical stories.
  That is what the Greeks called MYTHOS — narrative as a vehicle for
  truth."

✋ [Quick verbal check — point to a student at random and ask: 'Give me
   a character and one of their TRAITS.' Go around to 4-5 students.
   Fast pace, low stakes, just warming up the vocabulary.]

SLIDE 4 — STATIC VS. DYNAMIC: THE CORE DISTINCTION
[Duration: ~5 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

"Now let's go deeper on the question from the warm-up: did your character
CHANGE? This is the distinction between STATIC and DYNAMIC characters —
and understanding it unlocks how ALL stories are built."

[Write STATIC and DYNAMIC on the board, or point to the slide]

"A STATIC character stays essentially the same from beginning to end.
Their core values, their personality, their worldview — unchanged.
This does NOT mean they're boring or unimportant."

"Example: Levi Ackerman in Attack on Titan. He begins as a ruthless,
emotionally closed-off soldier. He ends as a ruthless, emotionally
closed-off soldier. He does not change. BUT — he reveals more depth
over time. We understand WHY he is the way he is. He functions as a
fixed point of competence and moral clarity around which more dynamic
characters orbit."

💡 [CRITICAL NUANCE — say this carefully]:
"Here's the distinction that trips up even college students: a character
CAN experience enormous pain, loss, and trauma without having a dynamic
arc. Change in CIRCUMSTANCES is not the same as change in CHARACTER.
If Levi loses people he cares about but responds in exactly the same way
he always has — stoic, determined, relentless — he is static. His
situation changed. His CHARACTER did not. The test for a dynamic arc is
this question: 'Does the character make a different CHOICE at the end
than they would have made at the beginning, because of what they learned
or suffered?' If yes, they're dynamic. If no — static."

"A DYNAMIC character undergoes genuine internal transformation.

Example: Shoto Todoroki in My Hero Academia. He BEGINS the series as
someone who has rejected half of his identity — the fire side inherited
from his abusive father. He refuses to use it. He has built walls to
protect himself from his own pain. By the end of his arc, he chooses —
deliberately, freely — to use both his fire AND his ice. That choice is
the moment his arc completes. He is no longer the person who rejected
himself. He becomes someone who has integrated his whole identity."

💡 [CONNECT TO REAL LIFE]:
"Here's what makes great character arcs emotionally devastating: we
recognize ourselves in them. Every human being has a wound — something
in their past that shaped who they became, usually something they are
still working through. The reason we cry at character arcs is not that
we feel sorry for them. It is that watching them work through their wound
reminds us of our OWN. Great anime is essentially a safe space to
process the hardest parts of being alive."

✋ [Ask the class: 'Based on what we just learned — is YOUR character
   from the warm-up STATIC or DYNAMIC? Has your answer changed from
   what you originally thought? Take 30 seconds — update your notebook
   if needed.']

SLIDE 5 — DEEP DIVE: THE ANATOMY OF A CHARACTER ARC
[Duration: ~6 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

"Let's put the arc under a microscope. Every complete character arc
has a structure — and once you see it, you see it everywhere."

"The arc has THREE stages. Write these down."

"STAGE 1: THE ORDINARY WORLD — Who the character is BEFORE the story
forces them to change. This is not just background information. It is a
carefully constructed portrait of a person's FLAW — the thing they
believe, or the fear they carry, that will be challenged by everything
that happens next.

  → Izuku Midoriya in Season 1: A boy with no powers in a world built
    around powers. His wound is shame — the belief that he is not enough,
    that he was born fundamentally inadequate. His entire arc is the
    gradual dismantling and rebuilding of that belief."

"STAGE 2: THE CRISIS — The point where the story confronts the character
with the ONE thing they cannot handle without changing. This is sometimes
called the DARK NIGHT OF THE SOUL. The old version of the character cannot
survive what is happening. They must break and rebuild, or they fail.

  → For Todoroki, the crisis is his fight with Midoriya at the Sports
    Festival. Midoriya refuses to let him coast on just his ice. He
    screams at him: 'Your power IS your own.' That moment cracks the wall.
    Todoroki breaks. And from the break comes the fire."

"STAGE 3: THE TRANSFORMATION — The character makes a new CHOICE that
would have been impossible in Stage 1. They have changed. The world
around them may or may not reflect that change — but internally, they
are a different person.

  → The transformation is always shown through action, never through
    a character simply saying 'I have changed.' This is one of the most
    important rules in all of screenwriting: SHOW DON'T TELL. We see
    Todoroki use his fire. We don't need him to make a speech about it."

💡 [LITERARY THEORY DEPTH — only use this if energy is high]:
"This three-stage arc structure is not invented for anime. It maps directly
onto what mythologist Joseph Campbell called THE HERO'S JOURNEY — a pattern
he identified in myths, legends, and stories across every culture in human
history. Campbell argued that this pattern resonates so universally because
it mirrors the internal psychological process of genuine human growth.
In psychology, the same pattern appears in grief therapy, addiction recovery,
and trauma processing. The 'crisis' is what psychologists call ADAPTIVE
BREAKDOWN — the moment where old coping mechanisms fail and new ones must
be built. Your favorite anime character is, at some deep level, enacting
one of the oldest patterns in human storytelling. That's why they feel real."

[Write the three stages on the board with arrows between them:
ORDINARY WORLD → CRISIS → TRANSFORMATION]

"I want you to hold onto this diagram. We're going to use it in the
next activity."

SLIDE 6 — GUIDED ANALYSIS: MAP YOUR CHARACTER'S ARC
[Duration: ~8 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

"Now it's your turn. You're going to do a real character analysis —
the exact same kind of analysis that film critics, anime reviewers,
and literature professors write about for a living."

"Go back to the character you picked in the warm-up. In your notebook,
draw three boxes side by side and label them:
  BOX 1: ORDINARY WORLD
  BOX 2: CRISIS
  BOX 3: TRANSFORMATION"

"Fill in each box for your character. Here's what goes in each one:"

  BOX 1 — Ordinary World:
  'What is their FLAW or WOUND at the beginning?
   What do they believe about themselves or the world
   that turns out to be incomplete or wrong?'

  BOX 2 — Crisis:
  'What is the moment — the scene, the fight, the loss —
   that forces them to confront that flaw?
   What happens that their old self cannot handle?'

  BOX 3 — Transformation:
  'What is different about them at the end?
   What choice do they make that proves they have changed?
   What would the OLD version of them have done instead?'

"If your character is STATIC — great. In BOX 2, write: 'No major internal
crisis.' In BOX 3 write: 'No transformation — but describe what they REVEAL
over the course of the story that we didn't know at the start.'"

✋ [Give students 4-5 minutes to write. Circulate actively — look at what
   they're writing, ask questions: 'What's their wound?', 'What was the
   crisis moment?', 'How would their Season 1 self have reacted differently?']

[PROMPTS FOR STUDENTS WHO ARE STUCK]:
"If you can't name the wound — think about what your character is AFRAID
of, or what they feel ASHAMED of. That's almost always the wound."

"If you can't name the crisis — think about the saddest or most intense
moment in the series for that character. That's usually it."

"If you can't name the transformation — think about the LAST big choice
your character makes. What does it tell you about who they've become?"

[After writing time is up, do a quick pair-share]

✋ [Have students briefly share their three boxes with a partner. Then ask
   2-3 volunteers to share their analysis with the class. When they share,
   ask one follow-up question: 'What theme do you think this arc is
   exploring? What big human truth is it about?']

💡 [SYNTHESIZE after sharing]:
"Notice what's happening here. You started by saying 'I like this
character.' Now you can say: 'This character has a wound rooted in
[specific fear or shame]. The story puts pressure on that wound through
[specific event]. And their transformation reveals a theme about [human
truth].' That is literary analysis. That is the kind of thinking that
earns you an A in an English class, gets you into film school, and
lets you write compelling stories of your own."

SLIDE 7 — THE IRREPLACEABILITY TEST
[Duration: ~5 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

"Step 3 of the warm-up asked a very specific question that I want to
come back to now. The question was: 'Would the story be the same
without this character? Why or why not?'"

"This is what professional writers call the IRREPLACEABILITY TEST.
It is one of the most useful tools for evaluating a character."

"A character passes the irreplaceability test if — and ONLY if — removing
them would require the STORY ITSELF to fundamentally change. Not just
a few scenes. The STORY."

"Let's test it with some examples."

"Could you remove Rock Lee from Naruto? Think about what Rock Lee
REPRESENTS in the story. He exists to prove a specific argument: that
hard work can equal or exceed natural talent. He is the living thesis
statement of that theme. Remove Rock Lee, and the story loses one of its
most important ideas. He passes the test."

"Could you remove a random background ninja from Naruto? Yes — easily.
The story doesn't change. That character fails the test. In writing,
we call these FUNCTIONAL characters — they exist to fill a role but
are interchangeable."

💡 [ELEVATE THE CONCEPT]:
"Here's the deeper principle: a well-designed character is not just
a person in a story. They are an ARGUMENT. Every major character
embodies a specific WORLDVIEW or PHILOSOPHY — a set of beliefs about
how the world works and what matters. The story is structured as a
DEBATE between those worldviews.

In My Hero Academia, Izuku believes in saving everyone — no exceptions.
All For One believes power is purely for domination. Todoroki believed
his power was his father's, not his own. Each of these is a different
philosophy. The story is a debate between them, and the 'winner' of that
debate at the end is how the story communicates its theme.

A character who passes the irreplaceability test is one whose worldview
is NECESSARY for the story's argument to be complete."

✋ [Discussion question — open to the class]:
"Does YOUR character from the warm-up pass the irreplaceability test?
What argument does their existence make? What worldview do they represent?
Take 90 seconds and add a sentence to your notebook:
'This character represents the idea that _______.'"

[Take 2-3 responses. Affirm all of them — this is high-level thinking
and students should feel proud for attempting it.]


━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
SLIDE 8 — TALK TIME: THE BEST AND WORST CHARACTER YOU'VE SEEN
[Duration: ~5 minutes]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

"This is your critical thinking time. I want you to do something that
literary critics do: compare a STRONG example to a WEAK one."

"Think of the best-written character you've ever seen in anime. Now think
of the worst-written character you've ever seen in anime — a character
who felt flat, boring, or fake."

"Don't hold back. This is a safe space for honest opinions."

✋ [Open discussion — 3 minutes. Encourage students to use the vocabulary:
   traits, motivation, arc, conflict, theme. If they don't use it naturally,
   prompt: 'What was their MOTIVATION?' / 'Did they have an ARC?']

[Listen for these patterns and name them when you hear them:]

"When you say a character felt FLAT — that's usually because they had
one trait and no internal conflict. A villain who is evil for no reason
has no internal conflict. They fail the motivation test."

"When you say a character felt FAKE — that's usually because their
choices didn't follow logically from who they were established to be.
In writing, we call that CHARACTER INCONSISTENCY. It breaks immersion
because our brains are extremely sensitive to social inconsistency —
we evolved to detect when people are acting 'out of character' because
it was a survival signal."

"When you say a character felt BORING even though they were clearly
important — that might be because their arc was too CLEAN. A character
who has a wound, gets challenged, transforms perfectly, and ends up
happy... can feel unrealistically smooth. Real growth is messy, partial,
and sometimes tragic. That's why characters like Eren Yeager and Shinji
Ikari are so controversial — they are profoundly, uncomfortably FLAWED
transformations, and that realism disturbs us."

💡 [CRITICAL MEDIA LITERACY MOMENT]:
"Here's a question worth sitting with: Why does it matter if characters
are well-written? It's 'just' entertainment, right?

Actually, no. Psychologists have documented that we process fictional
characters through the SAME neural pathways we use to process real
people. When you experience a character's arc, your brain files it as
real social experience. The lessons a character learns become — on some
level — lessons you have 'lived.' This is why stories are one of the
most powerful tools for building empathy that has ever existed.

When a character is poorly written — especially a character from a
marginalized group — it doesn't just make a bad story. It reinforces
false and harmful models of real human beings. Good character writing
is not just a craft concern. It's an ethical one."

================================================================================
APPENDIX A — COMMON DISCUSSION PITFALLS & HOW TO HANDLE THEM
================================================================================

PITFALL: Student says 'I don't know' when asked about a character's motivation
  Handle: Ask them 'What does this character do in almost every episode?'
          Then: 'Why do you think they keep doing it?' The behavior usually
          points to the motivation. Let them work backward from action to reason.

PITFALL: Student says a character is 'just cool' with no deeper analysis
  Handle: Say 'I believe you! Now help me understand — what makes them cool
          specifically? Is it how they fight? How they talk to people? How they
          handle loss?' Drilling into specifics almost always surfaces traits
          and motivation.

PITFALL: Student argues passionately that a character IS dynamic but has
         evidence only of changed circumstances, not changed character
  Handle: Use the test: 'At the end of the series, if this character faced
          the exact same situation as Episode 1 — would they make the
          SAME choice or a DIFFERENT one? That's how we know if they changed.'

PITFALL: Student struggles to name a 'wound' for their favorite character
  Handle: Ask: 'What is something this character is ashamed of, or
          something they lost, or something they were told about themselves
          that they believe but maybe shouldn't?' This reframes wound
          from 'tragic backstory' to 'a belief that limits them.'

PITFALL: Conversation becomes too focused on plot summary instead of analysis
  Handle: Gently interrupt and redirect: 'That's great context — now
          let's connect it: what does that moment tell us about who
          they ARE? What trait does it reveal?'

APPENDIX B — EXTENSION QUESTIONS FOR ADVANCED STUDENTS
================================================================================

1. Can an ANTAGONIST have a better character arc than the protagonist?
   [Answer: Absolutely. Some of the greatest arcs in anime belong to
   villains — Vegeta in DBZ, Zuko in Avatar, Gaara in Naruto. In fact,
   a compelling antagonist almost always has a MIRROR arc to the hero:
   they face the same wound but make opposite choices. That mirroring
   is what makes a great villain feel inevitable and tragic rather
   than just evil.]

2. What's the difference between a character CHANGING and a character
   being INCONSISTENT?
   [Answer: Change is earned — it emerges from specific experiences the
   story shows us. Inconsistency is unexplained — a character acts
   differently from how they've been established with no story reason.
   The former deepens trust in the character; the latter breaks it.]

3. Can a story have NO dynamic characters and still be great?
   [Answer: Yes. Many comedies, mysteries, and thrillers use static
   protagonists by design. What changes in those stories is the
   SITUATION, not the character. The interest comes from watching
   a fixed personality collide with an increasingly complex world —
   think Sherlock Holmes, or many slice-of-life anime.]

4. What is a FOIL character, and how does it enhance the protagonist's arc?
   [Answer: A foil is a character designed specifically to contrast with
   another — usually the protagonist. By placing them side by side, the
   writer makes both characters' traits more visible. Bakugo is Midoriya's
   foil: same starting goal, radically different philosophy. Their contrast
   makes both arcs richer and clarifies the story's argument about what
   kind of hero to be.]

5. Is it possible to care too much about characters who don't exist?
   [Answer: This is a genuine philosophical question with a psychological
   answer — research on 'parasocial relationships' shows that our brains
   form real emotional bonds with fictional characters, and those bonds
   have real effects on our wellbeing, values, and social behavior.
   The ethical and psychological implications of deep investment in
   fiction is one of the most interesting conversations in media studies.]
